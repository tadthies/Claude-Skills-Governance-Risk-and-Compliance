Short answer: No. ISO 27001:2022 certification is strong evidence of a mature information security management system (ISMS) and will cover a large share of NIS2's technical and organizational measures, but it does not by itself satisfy NIS2. NIS2 is an EU legal directive (transposed into national law by each Member State) with its own scope, governance, reporting, and enforcement requirements that ISO 27001 does not fully address. Certification can significantly reduce your compliance effort and serve as supporting evidence, but you still need a dedicated gap assessment and additional work.

## Why ISO 27001 is not automatically "NIS2 compliant"

1. **Different nature of the two things.** ISO 27001 is a voluntary, certifiable management-system standard assessed by an accredited certification body. NIS2 is a binding EU legal directive transposed into national law, enforced by a national competent authority (NCA)/CSIRT, with statutory deadlines, penalties, and personal liability for management.
2. **Scope and applicability differ.** Your ISO 27001 certificate scope (which systems, sites, business units, and legal entities it covers) may not match the full scope of "essential" or "important" entity obligations under NIS2, which apply at the legal-entity level across your NIS2-relevant services, not just the certified scope.
3. **NIS2 has its own control baseline.** Article 21 of NIS2 lists ten mandatory categories of cybersecurity risk-management measures (risk analysis/policies, incident handling, business continuity/crisis management, supply chain security, security in acquisition/development/maintenance including vulnerability handling and disclosure, policies to assess effectiveness of measures, basic cyber hygiene and training, cryptography/encryption policies, HR security/access control/asset management, and use of MFA/continuous authentication/secure communications). ISO 27001's Annex A (2022) maps well onto most of these, but mapping is not the same as demonstrable, auditable compliance against NIS2's specific wording and any national implementing act's technical guidance (many countries issue their own detailed technical/implementing regulations, e.g., Germany's BSI, or NIS2-specific criteria).

## Likely gaps between an ISO 27001 ISMS and full NIS2 compliance

- **Regulatory registration and scoping.** NIS2 requires entities to determine whether they are "essential" or "important," register with the relevant NCA, and identify precisely which services/entities fall in scope. ISO 27001 has no equivalent registration step.
- **Incident notification timelines and content.** NIS2 mandates a strict, multi-stage incident reporting regime: early warning within 24 hours, incident notification within 72 hours, and a final report within one month, to the national CSIRT/NCA — with specific required content at each stage. ISO 27001 (Annex A 5.24–5.28) requires an incident management process but has no equivalent fixed statutory deadlines or regulator-facing reporting format. You will likely need new procedures, templates, and possibly tooling to meet these deadlines.
- **Supply chain and third-party risk depth.** NIS2 places explicit emphasis on supply chain security, including assessing the cybersecurity practices of direct suppliers/service providers and considering vulnerabilities specific to each supplier relationship (informed by ENISA coordinated risk assessments for critical sectors). ISO 27001 (A.5.19–5.23) covers supplier relationships but generally at a lighter, more generic level than NIS2 expects for essential/important entities.
- **Management body accountability and liability.** NIS2 explicitly requires management/board-level approval of cybersecurity risk-management measures, mandates cybersecurity training for management, and exposes management to personal liability and potential temporary bans from managerial roles for non-compliance. ISO 27001 requires top-management commitment (Clause 5) but does not carry the same statutory personal liability regime.
- **Business continuity/crisis management specifics.** NIS2 explicitly calls out crisis management and business continuity (backup management, disaster recovery) as a distinct control category. ISO 27001 covers this under A.5.29/A.5.30 and Clause 8, but you should verify your BCP/DR plans meet the granularity and testing cadence regulators will expect.
- **Vulnerability handling and coordinated disclosure.** NIS2 emphasizes vulnerability handling and disclosure practices, including interaction with national CSIRTs and, in some cases, the EU vulnerability database. ISO 27001 addresses technical vulnerability management (A.8.8) but not the coordinated disclosure/regulator interface NIS2 expects.
- **Use of specific technical measures.** NIS2 explicitly calls out multi-factor authentication, secure voice/video/text communications, and secured emergency communication systems within the organization — these are best-practice implied controls in ISO 27001 but not always explicitly mandated depending on your Statement of Applicability (SoA) exclusions.
- **Statement of Applicability exclusions.** Because ISO 27001 allows you to justify excluding certain Annex A controls based on risk assessment, any Annex A controls you've excluded or scoped down (e.g., certain supplier controls, cryptography, or physical security items) could create direct NIS2 gaps if NIS2 treats them as mandatory regardless of your risk assessment conclusions.
- **National-level implementing legislation.** Because NIS2 is a directive, each EU Member State transposes it with potentially different detail, additional sector-specific requirements, registration processes, fee structures, and audit/inspection regimes. Compliance is ultimately measured against your national law, not the directive text or your ISO certificate.
- **Evidence and audit trail differences.** ISO 27001 surveillance/recertification audits are periodic and sampling-based; NIS2 enforcement can include unannounced inspections, security audits ordered by the NCA, and requests for evidence of specific incidents or measures at any time — meaning your evidentiary trail needs to be more continuously "regulator-ready," not just "audit-week ready."
- **Penalties and enforcement mechanics differ**, which changes the risk calculus even where the underlying control exists — NIS2 fines can reach up to €10 million or 2% of global annual turnover for essential entities (whichever is higher), separate from any ISO certification body sanction (which is limited to suspension/withdrawal of certificate).

## Practical next steps

1. Confirm your classification (essential vs. important entity) and registration obligations under the national law of each Member State where you operate.
2. Map your current ISO 27001 Annex A controls and SoA against NIS2 Article 21's ten measure categories (and any national technical implementing rules) to identify explicit gaps and any risk-based exclusions in your SoA that NIS2 would not permit.
3. Build/validate a NIS2-specific incident reporting workflow (24-hour early warning, 72-hour notification, 1-month final report) integrated with your existing incident management process.
4. Strengthen supply chain risk assessment procedures to match NIS2's expected depth, including supplier-specific risk profiles.
5. Formalize management-body approval, oversight, and training on cybersecurity risk measures, and document this clearly (board minutes, training records).
6. Review business continuity/crisis management and vulnerability disclosure processes against NIS2 expectations.
7. Engage legal/compliance counsel familiar with your relevant national transposition law, since exact obligations, deadlines, and enforcement details vary by country.

In short: treat ISO 27001 certification as a strong foundation and a major accelerator for NIS2 readiness — not as a substitute for it. A formal gap assessment mapping your ISMS scope and SoA against NIS2's Article 21 requirements and your national implementing legislation is the necessary next step.
