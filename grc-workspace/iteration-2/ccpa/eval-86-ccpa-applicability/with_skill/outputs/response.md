# CCPA/CPRA Applicability and Core Obligations Analysis

## Are You Subject to CCPA/CPRA?

**Yes — your company is subject to CCPA/CPRA.** Here is the precise legal basis.

---

## The Threshold Test: Any One Is Sufficient

Under Cal. Civ. Code §1798.140(d), CCPA/CPRA applies to a **for-profit business** that does business in California and meets **at least one** of three thresholds. You do not need to satisfy all three — a single qualifying criterion is enough to bring your company within scope.

The three thresholds are:

1. **Annual gross revenues exceeding $25 million** (in the preceding calendar year)
2. **Annually buys, sells, receives, or shares the personal information (PI) of 100,000 or more consumers or households**
3. **Derives 50% or more of annual revenues from selling or sharing consumers' PI**

---

## Which Threshold(s) You Meet

### Threshold 1 — Revenue: **MET**

Your company reports **$30 million in annual gross revenues**, which exceeds the $25 million threshold. This single fact is sufficient to trigger CCPA/CPRA compliance obligations, regardless of how many customers you serve or whether you sell personal information.

### Threshold 2 — Volume: **Not Met (but close)**

You collect PI from approximately **80,000 California customers per year**, which falls below the 100,000 consumer/household threshold. Threshold 2 is therefore not independently satisfied.

### Threshold 3 — Revenue from PI: **Likely Not Met**

As an e-commerce company, your primary revenue likely comes from product sales rather than selling or sharing consumer PI. Unless more than half of your $30M in annual revenues comes specifically from selling or sharing PI to third parties, Threshold 3 does not apply to you.

### Bottom Line

You meet **Threshold 1** based on your $30M annual revenue. CCPA/CPRA applies in full. The fact that you do not meet Thresholds 2 or 3 is irrelevant — satisfaction of any single threshold is sufficient under the statute.

## Core Compliance Obligations

Once subject to CCPA/CPRA, your company must fulfill obligations across several domains.

### 1. Privacy Notice at Collection

At or before the time you collect personal information from California consumers, you must provide a **Privacy Notice at Collection** (also called a "just-in-time" notice) that discloses:

- The **categories of PI** being collected (e.g., identifiers, commercial information, internet activity)
- The **purposes** for which each category is collected and used
- **Whether PI is sold or shared** with third parties
- **Retention periods** (or the criteria used to determine them) — this is a CPRA addition effective January 1, 2023
- A **link to your full privacy policy**

This notice must appear at every collection point — checkout forms, account registration pages, newsletter sign-ups, and any other data-gathering touchpoint.

### 2. Privacy Policy

You must maintain a **comprehensive, publicly available Privacy Policy** (typically linked in your website footer) that covers:

- All **categories of PI collected** in the past 12 months
- The **purposes** for collecting each category
- **Categories of third parties** to whom PI is disclosed
- A complete description of **consumer rights** under CCPA/CPRA (see below)
- Contact information for **submitting consumer requests**
- A prominent **"Do Not Sell or Share My Personal Information"** link (or section) if you sell or share PI
- A **"Limit the Use of My Sensitive Personal Information"** link or section if you use SPI beyond the purposes necessary for the services requested
- The **date the policy was last updated**

Your privacy policy must be updated at least annually.

### 3. Consumer Rights Program

You must establish operational processes to honor the following consumer rights within the statutory deadlines:

| Right | Deadline |
|---|---|
| **Right to Know** (categories and specific pieces of PI collected) | 45 days from verified request; one 45-day extension allowed with notice |
| **Right to Delete** (PI you hold about the consumer) | 45 days from verified request; one 45-day extension allowed with notice |
| **Right to Correct** (inaccurate PI — CPRA addition) | 45 days from verified request; one 45-day extension allowed with notice |
| **Right to Opt-Out of Sale/Sharing** | **Immediately upon request** — no delay is permitted |
| **Right to Limit SPI Use** (CPRA addition) | **15 business days** |
| **Right to Data Portability** | Included within the Right to Know; same 45-day deadline |
| **Right to Non-Discrimination** | Ongoing — cannot deny service, charge different prices, or provide degraded service to consumers who exercise rights |

**Verification requirement:** For Know, Delete, and Correct requests, you must verify the consumer's identity before responding. The level of scrutiny scales with the sensitivity of the information requested. You must offer at least two methods for submitting requests, including a toll-free phone number and, if you have a website, a web form.

### 4. Do Not Sell or Share — Opt-Out Link and GPC

If your company **sells or shares** PI with third parties (including for targeted/behavioral advertising, which CPRA defines as "sharing" even without money changing hands), you must:

- Display a **"Do Not Sell or Share My Personal Information"** link **on your homepage** and in your privacy policy
- **Honor Global Privacy Control (GPC) signals** — if a consumer's browser sends a GPC opt-out signal, you must treat it as a valid opt-out request immediately, without requiring the consumer to also click a link. This is a mandatory statutory obligation under CPRA, not optional.
- Honor opt-out requests from **authorized agents** (someone a consumer designates to act on their behalf)

If you use third-party advertising pixels, retargeting tags, analytics cookies, or share data with ad networks, this almost certainly triggers "sharing" under CPRA's definition, even if you receive no money for it.

### 5. Service Provider Contracts

If you engage **service providers** (vendors, processors, cloud providers, email platforms, analytics tools) that handle California consumers' PI on your behalf, you must have written contracts in place that:

- Limit the service provider to processing PI **only for the specified business purpose**
- **Prohibit** the service provider from selling or sharing the PI
- **Prohibit** use of the PI for the service provider's own commercial purposes
- Require the service provider to **honor consumer deletion requests** and notify subprocessors
- Grant you **audit rights** to verify compliance
- Require deletion or return of PI upon contract termination

Without these contractual protections, your vendors may be classified as "third parties" rather than service providers, potentially triggering a "sale" of PI.

### 6. Data Minimization and Purpose Limitation (CPRA)

Since January 1, 2023, CPRA requires that you collect only the PI that is **reasonably necessary and proportionate** to the purposes disclosed in your privacy notice. You may not collect more data than needed, and you may not use data for purposes incompatible with those disclosed at collection.

### 7. Sensitive Personal Information (SPI) — Special Handling

If you collect SPI — which includes account log-in credentials with passwords, precise geolocation, health or medical information, racial or ethnic origin, or biometric data — consumers have the right to **limit your use of SPI** to only what is necessary to provide the requested services. You must:

- Provide a **"Limit the Use of My Sensitive Personal Information"** link or equivalent mechanism
- Honor limit requests within **15 business days**
- Not use SPI for inferences, advertising, or secondary purposes beyond delivering the core service

### 8. Cybersecurity Audits and Risk Assessments (Effective January 1, 2026)

Starting January 1, 2026, the CPPA requires businesses whose PI processing presents **significant risk to consumer security** to conduct annual cybersecurity audits. Additionally, **risk assessments** must be completed before undertaking high-risk PI processing activities (such as profiling, targeted advertising, or processing large volumes of sensitive data), with documentation available for submission to the CPPA on request.

## Penalties for Non-Compliance

Non-compliance carries meaningful financial exposure:

- **Unintentional violations:** up to **$2,500 per violation**
- **Intentional violations:** up to **$7,500 per violation**
- **Violations involving minors' PI:** up to **$7,500 per violation**
- **Private right of action (data breaches only):** **$100–$750 per consumer per incident**, or actual damages if higher

The CPPA — the dedicated enforcement agency created by CPRA — enforces the law and can investigate without waiting for a consumer complaint.
