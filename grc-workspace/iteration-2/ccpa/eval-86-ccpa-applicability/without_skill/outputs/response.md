# CCPA/CPRA Applicability and Core Obligations

## Are You Subject to CCPA/CPRA?

**Yes, your company is subject to CCPA/CPRA.**

The California Consumer Privacy Act (CCPA), as amended and strengthened by the California Privacy Rights Act (CPRA, effective January 1, 2023), applies to for-profit businesses that do business in California and meet **at least one** of three threshold criteria. Your company meets at least one — and possibly two — of these thresholds.

---

## Threshold Analysis

Under CPRA (which updated CCPA's thresholds), a for-profit business is covered if it:

1. **Has annual gross revenues exceeding $25 million** (in the preceding calendar year), OR
2. **Buys, sells, or shares the personal information of 100,000 or more California consumers or households** per year, OR
3. **Derives 25% or more of its annual revenues from selling or sharing consumers' personal information**

### Thresholds Your Company Meets

**Threshold 1 — Revenue: YES, MET**
Your annual revenue of $30 million exceeds the $25 million threshold. This alone is sufficient to trigger CCPA/CPRA applicability. No other threshold needs to be met.

**Threshold 2 — Consumer Volume: NOT MET (currently)**
You collect personal information from approximately 80,000 California customers per year. The CPRA raised this threshold from 50,000 (under original CCPA) to 100,000. At 80,000 consumers, you fall below this threshold — but you should monitor this closely. If your customer base grows to 100,000 California consumers, you will independently satisfy this threshold as well.

**Threshold 3 — Revenue from Selling/Sharing PI: Likely not met, but verify**
Unless 25% or more of your $30M revenue comes from selling or sharing personal information (e.g., data brokerage activities), this threshold does not apply. Most e-commerce companies do not meet this threshold through ordinary operations.

### Key Takeaway on Thresholds
You are covered primarily because your revenue ($30M) exceeds $25M. The consumer volume threshold is close but not yet met. Both thresholds should be tracked annually, as CCPA/CPRA applicability must be reassessed each calendar year.

## Core Compliance Obligations

### 1. Privacy Notice / Privacy Policy

You must provide California consumers with a **Notice at Collection** and a comprehensive **Privacy Policy** that discloses:

- **Categories of personal information collected** (e.g., identifiers, commercial information, internet activity, geolocation data, inferences)
- **Purposes for collection and use** of each category
- **Categories of third parties** with whom you share or disclose personal information
- **Retention periods** (or criteria used to determine retention) for each category — this is a CPRA addition
- **Whether personal information is sold or shared** for cross-context behavioral advertising
- **Consumer rights** available under CCPA/CPRA and how to exercise them
- **Contact information** for submitting requests

The Notice at Collection must be provided **at or before the point of collection** — e.g., at account signup, checkout, or any other data collection touchpoint.

Your privacy policy must be updated **at least annually**.

### 2. Consumer Rights You Must Honor

CPRA grants California consumers the following rights, and you must establish processes to fulfill them:

#### Right to Know
Consumers may request disclosure of:
- The specific pieces of personal information you hold about them
- The categories of personal information collected
- The categories of sources from which it was collected
- The business or commercial purposes for collection
- The categories of third parties to whom it was disclosed

#### Right to Delete
Consumers may request deletion of their personal information. You must delete the data from your records and direct your service providers and contractors to do the same. Certain exceptions apply (e.g., completing a transaction, detecting security incidents, complying with legal obligations, internal uses aligned with the consumer's reasonable expectations).

#### Right to Correct (CPRA Addition)
Consumers may request correction of inaccurate personal information. This is new under CPRA and did not exist under the original CCPA.

#### Right to Opt-Out of Sale or Sharing
Consumers have the right to opt out of the **sale** of their personal information and the **sharing** of their personal information for cross-context behavioral advertising (e.g., targeted advertising through third-party ad networks). This applies even if no money changes hands — "sharing" for advertising purposes is regulated separately from "selling."

#### Right to Limit Use of Sensitive Personal Information (CPRA Addition)
Consumers may direct you to limit the use and disclosure of sensitive personal information (SPI) to only what is necessary to perform the requested service. Sensitive personal information includes:
- Social security numbers, driver's license numbers, or passport numbers
- Financial account credentials (account numbers plus access codes)
- Precise geolocation data
- Racial or ethnic origin, religious or philosophical beliefs, union membership
- Contents of mail, email, and text messages
- Genetic or biometric data processed to identify an individual
- Health or medical information
- Information about sex life or sexual orientation

#### Right of Non-Discrimination
You may not discriminate against consumers who exercise their CCPA/CPRA rights by denying goods or services, charging different prices, or providing a different level of quality — unless the difference is reasonably related to the value of the consumer's data.

#### Right to Data Portability (part of Right to Know)
When providing specific pieces of personal information upon request, you must deliver the data in a portable, readily usable format that allows the consumer to transmit it to another entity.

## Opt-Out Mechanisms

### "Do Not Sell or Share My Personal Information" Link

If you sell or share personal information (including through third-party advertising pixels or retargeting), you must:

- Post a **"Do Not Sell or Share My Personal Information"** link clearly and conspicuously on your homepage and privacy policy
- Honor opt-out requests without requiring account creation
- Process opt-out requests within **15 business days**
- Ensure opted-out consumers are not re-enrolled without their explicit consent for at least **12 months**

### Global Privacy Control (GPC) Compliance

Under CPRA, you must honor the **Global Privacy Control (GPC)** browser signal as a valid opt-out of sale and sharing. If a consumer's browser sends the GPC signal, you must treat it as an opt-out request and stop selling or sharing their personal information — **even without any affirmative action** from the consumer. This is a significant operational requirement affecting your web analytics, ad tech, and data sharing integrations.

### Sensitive Personal Information Opt-Out

If you use or disclose sensitive personal information beyond what is strictly necessary to perform requested services, you must provide a **"Limit the Use of My Sensitive Personal Information"** link (or combine it with the sale/sharing opt-out link). This link must also appear on your homepage.

### Opt-In for Minors

- **Consumers under 16**: You may not sell or share their personal information without **opt-in consent**
- **Consumers under 13**: A parent or guardian must provide opt-in consent

As an e-commerce company, if you have reason to know that customers under 16 are using your platform, you must implement age-gating and consent mechanisms accordingly.

## Response Deadlines for Consumer Rights Requests

You must establish a **verifiable consumer request** process. "Verifiable" means you must take reasonable steps to confirm the identity of the requestor before disclosing or deleting data.

| Right | Response Deadline | Extension Allowed? |
|---|---|---|
| Right to Know | **45 calendar days** from receipt of request | Yes — one 45-day extension if you notify the consumer, for a maximum of 90 days total |
| Right to Delete | **45 calendar days** from receipt of request | Yes — one 45-day extension with notice, 90 days total |
| Right to Correct | **45 calendar days** from receipt of request | Yes — one 45-day extension with notice, 90 days total |
| Right to Opt-Out of Sale/Sharing | **15 business days** from receipt of request | No extension permitted |
| Right to Limit Use of SPI | **15 business days** from receipt of request | No extension permitted |

You must provide **two or more** methods for consumers to submit requests, including (at minimum):
- A **toll-free telephone number** (if you operate a website)
- A **web form or interactive web page** accessible through your website

For online-only businesses with a direct relationship with consumers, a web form alone may suffice, but offering multiple channels is best practice.

## Additional Operational Requirements

### Data Processing Agreements

You must enter into **contracts with all service providers, contractors, and third parties** that receive personal information from you. These contracts must:
- Prohibit the service provider from selling or sharing the personal information
- Prohibit use of the data outside the specified business purpose
- Require the service provider to comply with applicable CCPA/CPRA obligations
- Grant you the right to audit the service provider's compliance

### Data Minimization (CPRA Addition)

You may only collect, use, retain, and share personal information that is **reasonably necessary and proportionate** to the disclosed purposes. You cannot collect more data than needed and cannot use data in ways incompatible with the purposes disclosed at collection.

### Purpose Limitation (CPRA Addition)

Personal information collected for one disclosed purpose cannot be used for a materially different, incompatible, or unrelated purpose without providing a new Notice at Collection and obtaining any required consent.

### Security Requirements

You must implement **reasonable security measures** appropriate to the nature of the personal information you collect. The California Attorney General has referenced the CIS Controls and other industry standards as benchmarks. A data breach caused by a failure to maintain reasonable security can expose you to private rights of action by affected consumers.

### Record-Keeping (CPRA Addition)

Businesses subject to CPRA must maintain records of consumer requests and responses for at least **24 months**. This is required for audit and regulatory purposes.

## Enforcement and Penalties

- The **California Privacy Protection Agency (CPPA)** — created by CPRA — has rulemaking and enforcement authority, independent of the California Attorney General
- **Civil penalties**: Up to **$2,500 per unintentional violation** and up to **$7,500 per intentional violation**
- **Private right of action**: Consumers may sue directly (without waiting for agency action) for certain **data breaches** involving non-encrypted, non-redacted personal information due to a failure of reasonable security. Statutory damages range from **$100 to $750 per consumer per incident**, or actual damages if greater
- **Cure period**: The CPPA may provide a 30-day cure period before initiating enforcement in some circumstances, but this is discretionary under CPRA (not guaranteed as it was under CCPA)

## Immediate Next Steps

1. **Audit your data flows**: Map all personal information collected, sources, purposes, retention periods, and third-party disclosures
2. **Update your Privacy Policy** to meet CPRA disclosure requirements
3. **Add Notice at Collection** to all data collection touchpoints (checkout, account creation, email signup, etc.)
4. **Implement opt-out mechanisms**: "Do Not Sell or Share My Personal Information" link and GPC signal compliance
5. **Build a consumer request intake process**: Web form, toll-free number, identity verification workflow, and ticketing system to track deadlines
6. **Audit your vendor contracts**: Ensure all service providers and contractors have compliant data processing agreements
7. **Review your ad tech stack**: Identify all third-party pixels, analytics tools, and retargeting vendors — these are common "sharing" scenarios that trigger opt-out obligations
8. **Train your team**: Customer service, marketing, IT, and legal staff need CCPA/CPRA awareness training
