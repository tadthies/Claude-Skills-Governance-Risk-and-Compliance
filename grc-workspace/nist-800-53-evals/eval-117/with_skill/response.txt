PART 1: AC-2(3) CONTROL DESCRIPTION AND REGULATORY CONTEXT
------------------------------------------------------------

Control: AC-2(3) — Account Management | Disable Accounts

Control Statement (NIST SP 800-53 Rev 5):
  "Disable accounts within [Assignment: organization-defined time period] when
  the accounts: (a) Have expired; (b) Are no longer associated with a user or
  individual; (c) Are in violation of organizational policy; or (d) Have been
  inactive for [Assignment: organization-defined time period]."

Baseline Assignment:
  - Low:      NOT required
  - Moderate: REQUIRED
  - High:     REQUIRED

Your ODV (Organization-Defined Value): 90 days of inactivity triggers mandatory
account disablement.

Why This Control Matters:
AC-2(3) is a foundational access control requirement because dormant accounts
represent one of the highest-probability attack vectors for credential-based
intrusions. Inactive accounts:
  - Are rarely monitored for anomalous logon attempts
  - May retain stale but valid credentials (unchanged passwords)
  - Provide unauthorized access if credentials are compromised
  - Violate the principle of least privilege (AC-6) by maintaining access for
    users who no longer require it
  - Can be exploited in lateral movement during a breach

The assessor's finding — 120 accounts inactive for over 120 days against your
stated ODV of 90 days — is a clear, objective, and documented OTS finding.
The violation exceeds your ODV by 30+ days and affects a significant population
(120 accounts), which elevates the risk severity.

Related Controls You Must Also Review:
  - AC-2 (base): Account management lifecycle — creation, review, modification,
    removal
  - AC-2(1): Automated system account management (Moderate/High)
  - AC-2(5): Inactivity logout (session-level; separate from AC-2(3))
  - AC-5: Separation of duties — verify disabled account holders had no
    conflicting privilege concentrations
  - IA-4: Identifier management — identifiers for inactive accounts must also
    be managed
  - CA-7: Continuous monitoring — your ConMon process should have caught this
  - CA-5: POA&M — document this finding immediately

PART 2: IMMEDIATE REMEDIATION STEPS
================================================================================

The remediation process has two parallel tracks: immediate operational
remediation (fix the condition now) and process remediation (fix why it
happened and prevent recurrence).

--- TRACK A: IMMEDIATE OPERATIONAL REMEDIATION (Days 1-7) ---

Step 1: Account Inventory and Validation (Day 1-2)
  - Pull a complete report of all 120 flagged accounts from your identity
    management system or Active Directory (or equivalent directory service).
  - For each of the 120 accounts, validate:
      a) Last logon timestamp (confirm inactivity period)
      b) Account type (user, service, privileged, shared, or system account)
      c) Current account status (enabled/disabled)
      d) Associated individual or function (is the account orphaned?)
      e) Group memberships and privilege level
  - Segregate the 120 accounts into sub-categories:
      - Standard user accounts inactive > 90 days
      - Privileged accounts inactive > 90 days (PRIORITY — disable FIRST)
      - Service accounts (do NOT disable without application impact analysis;
        coordinate with application owners)
      - Accounts for separated employees (disable immediately; may require
        coordination with HR and PS-4 controls)

Step 2: Prioritized Disablement (Day 2-5)
  - PRIORITY 1 (Day 2): Disable all privileged/admin accounts in the inactive
    set first. Privileged account compromise has the highest blast radius.
  - PRIORITY 2 (Day 3-4): Disable all standard user accounts confirmed inactive
    for > 90 days.
  - PRIORITY 3 (Day 4-5): For each service account, perform application impact
    analysis. Coordinate with system/application owners before disabling. If
    a service account is genuinely inactive (no application dependency), disable
    it. If active dependency exists, document the exception and escalate.
  - Document every account disabled, including: account name, account type,
    last logon date, disabled-by, disabled-on timestamp, and ticket/work order
    reference.

Step 3: Notification Protocol (Day 1-3, concurrent)
  - Notify account holders' managers (or HR for separated personnel) before
    disabling, where operationally feasible and where the delay does not extend
    the violation window beyond an acceptable period.
  - For accounts of active employees that are simply unused, give a documented
    24-48 hour notice before disablement per your access control policy.
  - For accounts of separated personnel, disable immediately and notify the
    responsible manager.

Step 4: Exception Handling (Day 3-5)
  - Any account that cannot be immediately disabled (e.g., undocumented service
    accounts with unresolved dependencies) must be:
      a) Logged as an outstanding exception in the POA&M milestone tracker
      b) Assigned an owner responsible for resolving the dependency
      c) Given a hard deadline (suggest 30 days maximum for service account
         resolution) with written approval from the ISSO and System Owner
  - Do NOT simply carry exceptions forward without documentation — each one
    must have an owner and a date.

--- TRACK B: ROOT CAUSE AND PROCESS REMEDIATION (Days 7-60) ---

Step 5: Root Cause Analysis (Day 7-14)
  Determine WHY 120 accounts were not disabled within the 90-day ODV. Common
  root causes include:
    a) Automated account review process was not configured, broke, or was
       disabled
    b) Reports were generated but not acted upon (process failure, not technical)
    c) Account review responsibility was unclear (no defined owner)
    d) Service desk or IT operations lacked an enforced workflow to disable
       flagged accounts
    e) The ODV was documented in the SSP but never operationalized in the
       directory service or IAM tool
    f) Exceptions were granted informally without documentation
  Document the root cause in the POA&M milestone record.

Step 6: Automated Controls Implementation (Day 14-45)
  Implement or repair the automated account management capability required by
  AC-2(1) (which is also a Moderate/High baseline requirement and may itself
  be OTS if automation was absent):
    - Configure Active Directory (or your IAM/IDM platform) to automatically
      flag accounts reaching 75 days of inactivity (15-day warning ahead of
      90-day threshold)
    - Configure automatic disablement at 90 days of inactivity
    - Establish automated notifications to account managers at 60 days and
      75 days
    - Test the automated process end-to-end in a non-production environment
      before deploying to production
    - Document the configuration settings as evidence for future assessments

Step 7: Policy and Procedure Update (Day 14-30)
  - Review and update the Access Control Policy (required by AC-1) to explicitly
    state the 90-day ODV and the automated enforcement mechanism
  - Update the Account Management Procedure to define:
      - Roles responsible for account review and disablement
      - Escalation path for accounts that cannot be automatically disabled
      - Quarterly manual review requirement as a backup to automation
      - Service account exception handling process
  - Ensure procedures reference the specific system and directory service
    configuration, not generic language

Step 8: Training and Awareness (Day 30-60)
  - Brief IT operations and help desk staff on the revised account management
    procedures
  - Document training completion records (supports AT-3)

Step 9: Verification and Evidence Collection (Day 45-60)
  - Run a post-remediation account report showing zero accounts inactive > 90
    days
  - Capture screenshots or exported reports from the directory service
    demonstrating the automated disablement configuration
  - Request a spot-check or mini-assessment from your ISSO or an independent
    reviewer to confirm the control is now Satisfied
  - Retain all remediation evidence in your compliance artifact repository

PART 4: ISSO AND SYSTEM OWNER SIGN-OFF REQUIREMENTS
================================================================================

Federal policy and SP 800-37 Rev 2 establish clear accountability requirements
for POA&M management. The following sign-offs are required:

1. ISSO Responsibilities and Sign-Off:
   - The ISSO is the day-to-day security point of accountability for this
     finding. The ISSO must:
       a) Review and validate the initial finding against the SAR
       b) Approve the POA&M entry before it is submitted to the AO
       c) Verify each milestone is completed and evidence is adequate
       d) Sign the POA&M closure memo when all milestones are complete
       e) Update the SSP AC-2(3) implementation narrative to reflect the
          remediated and now-operational control
       f) Certify the accuracy of the POA&M in monthly ConMon reports
   - ISSO signature is required on:
       - Initial POA&M entry (at submission)
       - Each monthly status update if milestones shift
       - Final POA&M closure memo

2. System Owner (SO) Responsibilities and Sign-Off:
   - The System Owner has ultimate accountability for the system's security
     posture and must:
       a) Acknowledge the finding and concur with the remediation plan
       b) Authorize the resource allocation (staff time, tooling changes)
       c) Accept any residual risk during the remediation window
       d) Co-sign the POA&M closure memo
       e) Ensure the updated Account Management Procedure is formally approved
   - SO signature is required on:
       - Initial POA&M entry concurrence
       - Root Cause Analysis memo
       - POA&M closure memo
       - Updated Access Control Policy (AC-1) if revised

3. Authorizing Official (AO) / AODR Notification:
   - The AO must be notified of the OTS finding and the POA&M entry.
   - If any accounts in the 120-account set are privileged accounts, the AO
     should be explicitly briefed given the elevated risk to the authorization
     boundary.
   - AO signature is NOT required for routine Moderate-risk POA&M entries
     UNLESS: (a) risk acceptance is invoked, (b) the finding affects a critical
     or high-impact component, or (c) agency policy requires AO concurrence.
   - If remediation cannot be completed within the scheduled completion date,
     a formal risk acceptance memorandum requiring AO signature must be
     prepared and executed.

4. Risk Acceptance (if needed):
   If any sub-item (e.g., service accounts with unresolved dependencies) cannot
   be remediated within the standard timeline, a Risk Acceptance must be
   documented separately, including:
     - Specific accounts or account types accepted under the risk acceptance
     - Business justification for the delay
     - Compensating controls in place during the acceptance period
     - Expiration date of the risk acceptance
     - ISSO signature, System Owner signature, and AO signature
   Risk acceptance is NOT a permanent closure of the POA&M item — the item
   remains open and must be reviewed at each ConMon cycle.

PART 6: CONTINUOUS MONITORING (ConMon) — PREVENTING RECURRENCE
================================================================================

To prevent this finding from recurring at the next assessment cycle:

1. Monthly ConMon Check: Include an account inactivity report in your monthly
   ConMon package. Run a query for accounts approaching 75 days and accounts
   at 90+ days. Review results with IT Operations and document actions taken.

2. Automated Alerting: Configure alerts (via SIEM or IAM platform) when any
   account exceeds 85 days of inactivity — providing a 5-day buffer to take
   manual action before the ODV is breached.

3. Quarterly Manual Review: Supplement automation with a quarterly manual
   account review (tie to CA-7 continuous monitoring tasks) that specifically
   covers:
     - Service accounts
     - Shared accounts
     - Privileged accounts
     - Any accounts excluded from automated review

4. Annual Control Assessment: Include AC-2(3) as a prioritized control in your
   annual assessment (or ConMon-based ongoing assessment) to validate that the
   automated process continues to function correctly.

5. POA&M Monthly Updates: Until this POA&M item is formally closed, provide
   milestone progress updates monthly. Include accounts remaining to be
   disabled, milestones completed, and any new exceptions identified.
