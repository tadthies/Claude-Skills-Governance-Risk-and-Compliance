NIST SP 800-53 Rev 5 — Multi-Factor Authentication Requirements for Federal Systems
Executive Order 14028 / OMB M-22-09 Phishing-Resistant MFA Guidance
==============================================================================

OVERVIEW
--------
Implementing multi-factor authentication (MFA) for a federal system involves several
controls within the IA (Identification and Authentication) family of NIST SP 800-53
Rev 5. The specific controls required depend on the system's FIPS 199 impact level
(Low, Moderate, or High) and the user population (privileged vs. non-privileged,
network vs. local access). Overlaid on the baseline requirements, Executive Order 14028
and OMB Memorandum M-22-09 impose additional mandates for phishing-resistant MFA that
go beyond the standard SP 800-53 baseline language.

This response addresses:
  1. The specific IA-2 control enhancements that mandate MFA
  2. Baseline assignments for each enhancement (L/M/H per SP 800-53B)
  3. EO 14028 and OMB M-22-09 phishing-resistant MFA requirements
  4. The PIV/FIDO2 vs. SMS/TOTP distinction
  5. SP 800-63B Authenticator Assurance Level (AAL) alignment
  6. Implementation guidance and SSP narrative considerations


PART 1 — SP 800-53 Rev 5 IA-2 CONTROLS THAT REQUIRE MFA
=========================================================

The base control IA-2 (Identification and Authentication — Organizational Users)
requires that the system uniquely identify and authenticate organizational users. MFA
is mandated through specific enhancements to IA-2. The relevant enhancements are:


1. IA-2(1) — Multi-Factor Authentication to Privileged Accounts
   -------------------------------------------------------------
   Baseline Assignment: LOW | MODERATE | HIGH (all three baselines)

   Control Statement:
   Implement multi-factor authentication for access to privileged accounts.

   Scope: All privileged (administrative) accounts, regardless of access method
   (network, local, or console). "Privileged" means accounts with elevated permissions
   such as system administrators, database administrators, and security administrators.

   Key Implementation Points:
   - Both factors must be from different categories: something you know, something you
     have, or something you are.
   - This enhancement applies to ALL privileged account access — not just network access.
   - Required at the LOW baseline, making it mandatory for every federal information
     system regardless of categorization.
   - Post-EO 14028, the acceptable authenticator types are further constrained (see
     Part 3 below).

   SP 800-53B Baseline: Required at Low, Moderate, and High.


2. IA-2(2) — Multi-Factor Authentication to Non-Privileged Accounts
   -----------------------------------------------------------------
   Baseline Assignment: LOW | MODERATE | HIGH (all three baselines)

   Control Statement:
   Implement multi-factor authentication for access to non-privileged accounts.

   Scope: All non-privileged (standard user) accounts. In the Rev 5 version of this
   enhancement, the requirement applies broadly — the historical limitation to "network
   access" from Rev 4 has been revised so that Rev 5 focuses on the account type
   rather than the access vector.

   Key Implementation Points:
   - MFA applies to the full population of organizational users, not just administrators.
   - Systems must ensure that all users authenticate with at least two factors prior to
     gaining access.
   - Required at the LOW baseline in SP 800-53 Rev 5, representing a significant
     strengthening from Rev 4 where IA-2(2) was only required at Moderate and above.

   SP 800-53B Baseline: Required at Low, Moderate, and High.


3. IA-2(6) — Access to Accounts — Separate Device
   ------------------------------------------------
   Baseline Assignment: HIGH only

   Control Statement:
   Implement multi-factor authentication for [Selection: local; network] access to
   [Selection: privileged accounts; non-privileged accounts] such that:
     (a) One of the factors is provided by a device separate from the system gaining
         access; and
     (b) The device meets [Assignment: organization-defined strength of mechanism
         requirements].

   Scope: High-impact systems only. This enhancement goes beyond IA-2(1) and IA-2(2)
   by requiring that at least one authentication factor be provided by a physically
   separate device — typically a hardware token, smart card, or mobile authenticator
   app on a separate device.

   Key Implementation Points:
   - The "separate device" requirement prevents an attacker who has compromised the
     workstation from also controlling the second factor.
   - PIV/CAC cards (smart cards) inherently satisfy this requirement because the
     cryptographic operations occur on the card itself, separate from the workstation.
   - FIDO2 hardware security keys (e.g., YubiKey) also satisfy this requirement.
   - Organization-defined strength of mechanism requirements should reference NIST SP
     800-63B Authenticator Assurance Level 3 (AAL3) for High-impact systems.

   SP 800-53B Baseline: Required at High only. Organizations with Moderate systems
   should consider this enhancement based on risk; it is also a strong candidate for
   supplemental control selection when threat modeling indicates insider threat or
   advanced persistent threat (APT) actors.


4. IA-2(8) — Access to Accounts — Replay Resistant
   -------------------------------------------------
   Baseline Assignment: MODERATE | HIGH

   Control Statement:
   Implement replay-resistant authentication mechanisms for access to [Selection:
   privileged accounts; non-privileged accounts].

   Scope: Moderate and High impact systems. This enhancement requires that the
   authentication mechanism cannot be successfully replayed by an adversary who
   captures the authentication exchange.

   Key Implementation Points:
   - Replay resistance is satisfied by authenticators that use nonces, timestamps, or
     cryptographic challenge-response protocols — meaning each authentication exchange
     produces a unique, time-limited credential.
   - PIV/CAC cards and FIDO2 keys inherently provide replay resistance through
     asymmetric cryptography.
   - One-time password (OTP) tokens (hardware TOTP) provide partial replay resistance
     because the OTP is time-limited, but they are susceptible to real-time phishing
     (attacker intercepts and immediately replays the OTP). See Part 3.
   - Kerberos with pre-authentication, TLS mutual authentication, and SAML assertions
     with short validity windows also satisfy replay resistance in specific deployment
     contexts.
   - SMS-based OTP does NOT provide meaningful replay resistance — it is vulnerable
     to SIM-swapping and SS7 protocol attacks, and it is not considered replay-
     resistant in the context of this control.

   SP 800-53B Baseline: Required at Moderate and High. Not included in Low baseline
   but may be selected as a supplemental control based on risk.


5. IA-2(12) — Acceptance of PIV Credentials
   ------------------------------------------
   Baseline Assignment: LOW | MODERATE | HIGH (all three baselines)

   Control Statement:
   Accept and electronically verify Personal Identity Verification (PIV) credentials
   from other federal agencies.

   Scope: All federal information systems. This control flows from Homeland Security
   Presidential Directive 12 (HSPD-12), which mandates government-wide use of PIV
   credentials (FIPS 201 compliant), and OMB M-11-11, which required all federal
   agencies to accept PIV credentials from employees of other agencies.

   Key Implementation Points:
   - The system must be technically capable of validating PIV credentials — this
     requires integrating with a PIV-capable identity provider or implementing a
     Certificate Validation Authority (CVA).
   - PIV validation involves checking:
       (a) Certificate path validation against the Federal PKI (FPKI) trust anchor
       (b) Certificate revocation (OCSP or CRL checks against the issuing CA)
       (c) Cardholder Unique Identifier (CHUID) validation or Card Authentication
           Certificate (CAC) validation
   - For cloud-hosted or SaaS systems, PIV acceptance is typically implemented through
     an agency identity provider (IdP) that federates PIV authentication (e.g., using
     SAML 2.0 or OIDC) rather than requiring direct PIV card integration at the
     application layer.
   - IA-2(12) is a universally required control: it appears in the Low, Moderate, and
     High baselines and cannot be scoped out for federal systems.

   SP 800-53B Baseline: Required at Low, Moderate, and High.

  - IA-12 [M/H] — Identity Proofing: requires in-person or supervised remote identity
    proofing at IAL2 or IAL3 (per SP 800-63A) before issuing authenticators.


PART 2 — RELATED AUTHENTICATOR MANAGEMENT CONTROL: IA-5
========================================================

While IA-2 establishes the MFA requirement, IA-5 (Authenticator Management) governs
how authenticators are issued, protected, revoked, and maintained. Key enhancements:

IA-5(2) — PKI-Based Authentication [Moderate/High]
   Requires:
   (a) Validation of certificates through construction and verification of a
       certification path to an accepted trust anchor including checking certificate
       status information.
   (b) Enforcement of authorized access to the private key.
   (c) Mapping of the authenticated identity to the account of the individual or group.
   (d) Implementation of a local cache of revocation data to support path discovery
       and validation for systems unable to access revocation information via the network.

This control is directly satisfied by PIV/CAC deployment. The private key must reside
in a hardware-protected cryptographic module (the PIV card itself, satisfying FIPS
140-3 Level 2 or higher requirements).

IA-5(1) — Password-Based Authentication [Low/Moderate/High]
   For systems that still use passwords as one factor, SP 800-63B Section 5 guidance
   applies:
   - Minimum 8 characters for memorized secrets (federal agencies typically set 15+)
   - No mandatory composition rules (per SP 800-63B — complexity requirements are
     counterproductive to security)
   - Check passwords against known compromised password lists
   - No mandatory periodic rotation unless compromise is suspected
   - Support paste functionality in password fields


PART 3 — EXECUTIVE ORDER 14028 AND OMB M-22-09 PHISHING-RESISTANT MFA REQUIREMENTS
====================================================================================

Executive Order 14028, "Improving the Nation's Cybersecurity" (May 12, 2021), Section 3,
mandates that federal agencies adopt a Zero Trust Architecture (ZTA) and modernize their
cybersecurity practices, including MFA. The implementing guidance is OMB Memorandum
M-22-09, "Moving the U.S. Government Toward Zero Trust Cybersecurity Principles"
(January 26, 2022).


Key EO 14028 Directives for MFA:
---------------------------------
Section 3(b)(i): Within 180 days, agencies must employ multi-factor authentication
where the authentication is resistant to phishing attacks.

The EO explicitly distinguishes between "multi-factor authentication" (the general
capability) and "phishing-resistant multi-factor authentication" (the specific security
property required for federal systems).


OMB M-22-09 Phishing-Resistant MFA Mandate:
--------------------------------------------
M-22-09 establishes the following specific requirements:

1. ALL federal staff, contractors, and partners must use phishing-resistant MFA.

2. Phishing-resistant MFA is defined as authentication that uses cryptographic
   techniques that prevent the authenticator output from being used to authenticate
   to a different party (i.e., the authenticator is bound to the specific service
   and cannot be replayed to a fraudulent site).

3. Deadline: Agencies must meet the MFA requirements within the fiscal year
   2024 milestones defined in the memorandum.

4. Approved phishing-resistant MFA methods (per M-22-09):
   APPROVED:
   - PIV/CAC smart cards (FIPS 201 / HSPD-12)
   - FIDO2 / WebAuthn hardware security keys (e.g., YubiKey, Google Titan Key)
   - Platform authenticators using FIDO2 (e.g., Windows Hello for Business with
     hardware TPM, Apple Face ID/Touch ID tied to Passkeys)

   NOT ACCEPTABLE as phishing-resistant MFA (even though they are "MFA"):
   - SMS-based one-time passwords (OTP) — vulnerable to SIM swapping, SS7 attacks,
     and real-time adversary-in-the-middle (AiTM) phishing
   - Voice call OTP — same vulnerabilities as SMS
   - Email-based OTP — susceptible to account compromise and AiTM phishing
   - TOTP/HOTP authenticator apps (e.g., Google Authenticator, Microsoft Authenticator
     in OTP mode) — the 6-digit code can be intercepted and replayed by AiTM tools
     (e.g., Evilginx2, Modlishka) in real time; NOT phishing-resistant
   - Push notifications without number matching — susceptible to MFA fatigue attacks

5. Internal-facing systems: Even internal/intranet systems must use phishing-resistant
   MFA for agency staff. M-22-09 does not permit exceptions for internal applications.

6. External-facing systems: Agencies must offer phishing-resistant MFA as an option
   to members of the public where technically feasible.


CISA Guidance — Implementing Phishing-Resistant MFA (2022):
------------------------------------------------------------
CISA has issued accompanying guidance reinforcing the M-22-09 requirements:
- Tier 1 (Preferred): Hardware PIV/CAC or FIDO2 hardware security keys
- Tier 2 (Acceptable for limited use cases): FIDO2 platform authenticators with
  biometric binding on government-managed devices
- NOT Acceptable: Any OTP-based method (SMS, TOTP, email) for systems subject to
  M-22-09

Key Distinction — Why TOTP Fails the Phishing-Resistant Test:
TOTP codes (the 6-digit codes from apps like Google Authenticator) are:
  - Not bound to the relying party (RP) — the same code could be submitted to a
    fraudulent site
  - Interceptable by real-time AiTM phishing proxies — the attacker captures the
    TOTP and immediately forwards it to the legitimate service
  - Not providing replay resistance in the M-22-09 / EO 14028 sense

PIV and FIDO2 are phishing-resistant because:
  - The cryptographic signature is bound to the specific origin/relying party
    (the domain or service). A signature generated for agency.gov cannot be used
    to authenticate to evil-agency.gov
  - The private key never leaves the hardware device
  - Authentication is a challenge-response protocol — there is no replayable
    shared secret


PART 4 — SP 800-63B AUTHENTICATOR ASSURANCE LEVELS (AAL) ALIGNMENT
====================================================================

NIST SP 800-63B (Digital Identity Guidelines — Authentication and Lifecycle Management,
Rev 3, June 2017; Rev 4 Draft under development) defines three Authenticator Assurance
Levels. Federal systems must map to the appropriate AAL based on impact level and risk:

AAL1 (Single Factor):
  - Not sufficient for any federal system covered by IA-2(1) or IA-2(2)
  - Suitable only for low-risk, public-facing informational applications without
    personal data

AAL2 (Multi-Factor, not necessarily phishing-resistant):
  - Requires two or more authentication factors
  - Approved authenticators include: OTP hardware/software tokens, PIV, FIDO2,
    look-up secrets, memorized secret + OTP
  - Satisfies SP 800-53 IA-2(1) and IA-2(2) at baseline level
  - Does NOT satisfy M-22-09 phishing-resistant MFA requirement if using TOTP/OTP
  - Appropriate for: Moderate impact systems not handling particularly sensitive data

AAL3 (Hardware-based, Phishing-Resistant, Verifier Impersonation Resistant):
  - Requires a hardware cryptographic authenticator
  - Requires verifier impersonation resistance (the authenticator is bound to the
    specific RP)
  - Approved authenticators: PIV/CAC, FIDO2 hardware security keys (roaming
    authenticators), FIDO2 platform authenticators with hardware TPM at specified
    assurance level
  - Satisfies M-22-09 phishing-resistant MFA requirement
  - Required for: High-impact systems, systems processing sensitive PII, privileged
    access to High systems, any system subject to IA-2(6)

Mapping Summary:
  System Baseline | Minimum AAL | Phishing-Resistant Required?
  Low             | AAL2        | Yes (per M-22-09, all federal systems)
  Moderate        | AAL2        | Yes (per M-22-09)
  High            | AAL3        | Yes (per M-22-09 and IA-2(6) requirement)

Note: M-22-09 effectively raises the floor for all federal systems to phishing-resistant
AAL2 or AAL3, superseding the plain AAL2 baseline reading of IA-2(1)/(2).

PART 6 — IMPLEMENTATION GUIDANCE FOR FEDERAL SYSTEMS
=====================================================

Step 1: Determine System Impact Level
  - Conduct FIPS 199 categorization and document in the system categorization form
  - High-impact systems must implement IA-2(1), IA-2(2), IA-2(6), IA-2(8), IA-2(12)
  - Moderate systems must implement IA-2(1), IA-2(2), IA-2(8), IA-2(12)
  - Low systems must implement IA-2(1), IA-2(2), IA-2(12)
  - All systems, regardless of baseline, must implement phishing-resistant MFA per M-22-09

Step 2: Select Phishing-Resistant Authenticator(s)
  Option A — PIV/CAC (preferred for federal employees and contractors):
    - Deploy PIV card readers at workstations
    - Integrate with agency Active Directory/LDAP using PKINIT or smart card logon
    - For web applications, implement PIV authentication via agency IdP (e.g., GSA
      Login.gov, agency ICAM solution)
    - Validate certificates against FPKI trust anchors
    - Implement OCSP checking for real-time revocation validation (configure caching
      for continuity of operations)

  Option B — FIDO2 Hardware Security Keys:
    - Deploy WebAuthn/FIDO2 at the application or IdP layer
    - Register keys to user accounts with attestation verification
    - Enforce enterprise-attestation or AAL3-level authenticators via RP policy
    - Suitable for developer workflows, API access, cloud services, and mobile
    - Document in SSP as supplemental phishing-resistant MFA method

  Option C — Hybrid (PIV primary, FIDO2 fallback):
    - PIV for primary workstation authentication
    - FIDO2 for remote access, cloud services, and mobile scenarios
    - Centralize both through agency IdP using SAML 2.0 or OIDC federation

Step 3: Address Legacy Systems
  - Legacy systems that cannot natively support PIV or FIDO2 must use a PIV-enabled
    IdP or proxy to front-end the authentication (e.g., Shibboleth, Okta, Azure AD
    with PIV federation)
  - Document compensating controls in the SSP if a short-term legacy exception exists
  - Include a Plan of Action and Milestones (POA&M) item with a specific remediation
    date for any system not yet meeting phishing-resistant MFA requirements

Step 4: Document in the SSP (System Security Plan)
  For each MFA control, the SSP narrative should address:

  IA-2(1) Implementation Statement (example structure):
    "The [System Name] implements multi-factor authentication for all privileged
    accounts using PIV/CAC smart cards (FIPS 201) as the primary authentication
    mechanism, supplemented by FIDO2 hardware security keys for privileged access
    from mobile or remote environments where PIV card readers are unavailable.
    Privileged account authentication is managed through [Agency IdP Name] using
    [SAML 2.0 / PKINIT / smart card logon]. Certificate validation is performed
    against the Federal PKI Common Policy Root CA. OCSP responses are cached locally
    for [X hours] to support operations during network outages. This implementation
    satisfies AAL3 requirements per NIST SP 800-63B and phishing-resistant MFA
    requirements per OMB M-22-09."

Step 5: Assessment Considerations (SP 800-53A)
  Assessors will examine, interview, and test:
  - Examine: SSP narratives, PIV/FIDO2 configuration documentation, IdP configuration,
    certificate validation policies, identity proofing records (IA-12)
  - Interview: ISSO, system administrator, identity management personnel
  - Test: Attempt authentication without second factor (should fail); verify PIV
    certificate validation (including revocation checking); test FIDO2 attestation
    enforcement; verify that SMS/TOTP methods are not enabled for accounts subject
    to phishing-resistant MFA requirements
