================================================================================
NIST SP 800-53 EXPERT RESPONSE
Topic: FedRAMP Moderate Authorization for ISO 27001:2022-Certified Organizations
================================================================================

QUESTION: We are preparing for our first FedRAMP Moderate authorization. We
currently have ISO 27001:2022 certification. What is the relationship between SP
800-53 Moderate controls and ISO 27001:2022 Annex A, what gaps will we likely
have, and how does the RMF process work for FedRAMP?

================================================================================
SECTION 1 — ISO 27001:2022 ANNEX A → SP 800-53 MODERATE CONTROL FAMILY MAPPING
================================================================================

ISO 27001:2022 and NIST SP 800-53 Rev 5 share substantial conceptual overlap,
but they differ in scope, prescriptiveness, and the specific controls each
requires. Your ISO 27001:2022 certification provides a meaningful starting point,
but it is not a substitute for the SP 800-53 Moderate baseline. The table below
maps each ISO 27001:2022 Annex A control domain to the corresponding SP 800-53
Rev 5 control family (or families) and identifies the nature of the alignment.

SUMMARY OF ALIGNMENT: Your ISO 27001:2022 certification confirms that you have a
functioning Information Security Management System (ISMS) covering policy,
risk assessment, supplier management, incident response, physical security,
business continuity, and access control at an organizational level. SP 800-53 Rev 5
derives from a common heritage with ISO 27001 and the two frameworks share roughly
70–80% conceptual overlap at the domain level.

However, alignment at the domain level does not equate to compliance at the
control level. SP 800-53 is significantly more prescriptive, more granular, and
carries U.S. federal-law specificity that ISO 27001 does not.

SECTION 2 — FEDRAMP MODERATE GAPS FOR ISO 27001:2022-CERTIFIED ORGANIZATIONS
================================================================================

The gaps below represent areas where ISO 27001:2022 certification typically
provides little or no coverage for FedRAMP Moderate requirements. These are
the highest-priority remediation items.

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
GAP 1 — FIPS 140-2 / FIPS 140-3 VALIDATED CRYPTOGRAPHIC MODULES
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
Controls: SC-13 (Cryptographic Protection), IA-7 (Cryptographic Module Auth)
Baseline: Moderate (required)

ISO 27001:2022 Annex A 8.24 requires "use of cryptography" and says
algorithms must be "appropriate." It does not mandate FIPS 140-validated
modules.

FedRAMP requirement: ALL cryptographic modules used in the system must be
validated to FIPS 140-2 or FIPS 140-3 (the successor standard). This applies
to:
  - TLS/HTTPS implementations (must use FIPS-approved cipher suites)
  - Data-at-rest encryption (SC-28; disk encryption libraries must be validated)
  - VPN / remote access tunnels (SC-8(1))
  - SSH implementations
  - Any key management system

Action items:
  - Inventory all cryptographic implementations across the system
  - Verify each against the NIST Cryptographic Module Validation Program (CMVP)
    at csrc.nist.gov/projects/cryptographic-module-validation-program
  - Replace or reconfigure any non-validated modules (e.g., OpenSSL must be
    used in FIPS mode with a FIPS 140-2/3 validated build)
  - Document FIPS validation certificate numbers in the SSP under SC-13

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
GAP 2 — PIV / PIV-INTEROPERABLE MULTI-FACTOR AUTHENTICATION (MFA)
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
Controls: IA-2(1), IA-2(2), IA-2(12), IA-8(1), IA-8(2), IA-8(4)
Baseline: Moderate (required)

ISO 27001:2022 Annex A 8.5 ("Secure Authentication") requires MFA for
privileged access but does not specify PIV (Personal Identity Verification)
cards as the required authenticator type.

FedRAMP/HSPD-12 requirement: Federal policy (OMB M-11-11, HSPD-12, OMB
M-19-17) mandates that all privileged users and all users accessing
federal systems use PIV credentials or PIV-Interoperable (PIV-I) credentials
for network authentication:

  - IA-2(1): MFA for privileged accounts (network access) — PIV required
  - IA-2(2): MFA for non-privileged accounts (network access) — PIV or
    PIV-I required for federal employee/contractor access
  - IA-2(12): PIV credentials accepted and verified
  - IA-8(1)/(2)/(4): Non-organizational users (public, contractors not
    issued PIV) must use NIST IAL/AAL-aligned credentials per SP 800-63B

Action items:
  - Implement PIV card authentication for all privileged users
  - For SaaS/cloud offerings, implement PIV-compatible identity federation
    via SAML or OIDC with a PIV-capable IdP (e.g., Azure AD Government,
    Okta FedRAMP, Ping Identity FedRAMP)
  - Document authenticator types, IAL/AAL levels, and PIV certificate
    validation procedures in SSP IA-2 narratives

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
GAP 3 — CONTINUOUS MONITORING (CONMON) FREQUENCIES
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
Controls: CA-7 (Continuous Monitoring), RA-5 (Vulnerability Monitoring),
          SI-2 (Flaw Remediation), CM-6 (Configuration Settings)
Baseline: Moderate (required)

ISO 27001:2022 requires vulnerability management and monitoring but does not
specify frequencies. FedRAMP ConMon requirements are prescriptive:

  Activity                          | FedRAMP Frequency
  ----------------------------------|-------------------
  OS vulnerability scans            | Monthly
  Web application vulnerability scans| Monthly
  Database vulnerability scans      | Weekly
  Annual 3PAO control assessment    | Annual
  Annual penetration test (3PAO)    | Annual
  POA&M updates                     | Monthly
  Inventory updates                 | Monthly
  Significant Change Requests       | Before major changes

Remediation deadlines (FedRAMP):
  - Critical vulnerabilities: 30 days
  - High vulnerabilities: 90 days
  - Moderate vulnerabilities: 180 days
  - Low vulnerabilities: 1 year (or risk acceptance)

Action items:
  - Implement automated vulnerability scanning at required FedRAMP frequencies
  - Stand up a monthly POA&M management process
  - Procure a 3PAO-accredited assessor for annual assessment and penetration test
  - Document ConMon strategy in CA-7 SSP narrative with specific frequencies
    and responsible parties
  - Establish a FedRAMP Significant Change Request process

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
GAP 4 — US-CERT / CISA INCIDENT REPORTING
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
Controls: IR-6 (Incident Reporting), IR-6(1), IR-7, IR-8
Baseline: Moderate (required)

ISO 27001:2022 Annex A 5.26 ("Response to information security incidents")
and 5.27 require incidents be managed and lessons learned, but reporting
timelines are organization-defined. ISO 27001 does not require reporting to
external government bodies.

FedRAMP incident reporting requirements (OMB M-17-25, US-CERT Federal
Incident Notification Guidelines):
  - All security incidents affecting federal data must be reported to US-CERT
    (now CISA) within 1 hour of discovery for critical/major incidents
  - Monthly operational reports to FedRAMP PMO and agency Authorizing Official
  - Incident handling procedures must reference the US-CERT reporting portal
    and use US-CERT severity categories (CAT 0 through CAT 6)

FedRAMP IR-6 ODV (Organization-Defined Value): Report within 1 hour to
FedRAMP PMO and agency customer(s); submit US-CERT notification within
1 hour for CAT 1/2 incidents.

Action items:
  - Update IR policy and procedures to include US-CERT/CISA reporting
    obligations and timelines
  - Train incident response team on FedRAMP-specific reporting procedures
  - Establish secure communications channel with agency customers for
    incident notification
  - Document IR-6 SSP narrative with specific reporting timelines and
    escalation procedures
  - Include IR-8 (Incident Response Plan) that references FedRAMP IR
    Playbook template

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
GAP 5 — THIRD-PARTY ASSESSMENT ORGANIZATION (3PAO) REQUIREMENT
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
Controls: CA-2 (Control Assessments), CA-8 (Penetration Testing)
Baseline: FedRAMP-specific requirement (not in standard SP 800-53B baseline)

ISO 27001:2022 requires internal audits (Clause 9.2) and management review
(Clause 9.3), and certification body audits. However, the ISO certification
body (CB) auditor is not equivalent to a FedRAMP 3PAO.

FedRAMP requirement: All assessments must be conducted by an accredited 3PAO:
  - 3PAOs must be accredited by either:
    a. American Association for Laboratory Accreditation (A2LA) under R311
    b. FedRAMP PMO directly (for select organizations)
  - The 3PAO conducts the initial assessment (producing the SAR) and all
    annual assessments thereafter
  - 3PAOs must be independent from the CSP — internal teams cannot serve
    as the 3PAO

Action items:
  - Identify and engage an accredited 3PAO early in the process
    (list available at marketplace.fedramp.gov)
  - Budget for initial 3PAO assessment (typically 6–18 weeks engagement)
    and recurring annual assessments
  - Provide 3PAO with a complete readiness package: SSP, policies,
    architecture diagrams, system inventory, prior audit evidence
  - Consider engaging 3PAO for a FedRAMP Readiness Assessment (FRA)
    before the full assessment to identify gaps

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
GAP 6 — FEDRAMP ORGANIZATION-DEFINED VALUES (ODVs)
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
Controls: Multiple — AC-2(3), AC-7, AU-11, IA-5(1), SI-2, CA-7, and others
Baseline: FedRAMP-specific parameter values

ISO 27001:2022 leaves most operational parameters to the organization
(e.g., password complexity, log retention, account lockout thresholds).
FedRAMP prescribes specific ODV values that override organization discretion.

Key FedRAMP ODV requirements for Moderate systems:

  Control   | Parameter                        | FedRAMP Required Value
  ----------|----------------------------------|------------------------
  AC-2(3)   | Disable inactive accounts after  | 90 days
  AC-7      | Account lockout threshold        | 3 invalid attempts
  AC-7      | Lockout duration                 | 30 minutes (or admin unlock)
  AU-11     | Audit log retention              | 90 days online; 1 year archived
  IA-5(1)   | Minimum password length          | 15 characters
  IA-5(1)   | Password complexity              | Upper, lower, number, special
  IA-5(1)   | Password history                 | 24 passwords remembered
  IA-5(1)   | Maximum password age             | 60 days (privileged);
            |                                  | No max for PIV/MFA accounts
  RA-5      | Vulnerability scan frequency     | Monthly (OS/web); Weekly (DB)
  SI-2      | Patch critical vulns within      | 30 days
  SI-2      | Patch high vulns within          | 90 days
  CA-7      | ConMon frequency                 | See Section 3 above

Action items:
  - Conduct an ODV gap analysis comparing your current operational parameters
    against FedRAMP-required values
  - Update technical configurations (AD Group Policy, SIEM retention settings,
    vulnerability scanner schedules) to meet FedRAMP ODVs
  - Document all ODVs explicitly in the SSP; FedRAMP reviewers specifically
    check that ODV parameters are populated (not left as "[organization-defined]")

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
GAP 7 — FEDRAMP SSP TEMPLATE AND DOCUMENTATION FORMAT
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
Controls: PL-2 (System Security Plan), CA-2, CA-7
Baseline: FedRAMP-specific requirement

ISO 27001:2022 requires a Statement of Applicability (SoA) and documented
ISMS policies, but does not specify SSP format.

FedRAMP requirement: CSPs must use the official FedRAMP SSP template
(available at fedramp.gov/documents-templates). The template is ~350+ pages
when fully completed and includes:

  - System description (architecture, boundaries, data flows)
  - FIPS 199 categorization determination
  - All 325+ Moderate baseline controls with implementation narratives
  - Responsible roles for each control
  - Implementation status (Implemented / Partially Implemented / Planned /
    Not Applicable)
  - Control inheritance designations (system-specific vs. inherited from CSP,
    IaaS/PaaS provider, or common control provider)
  - Customer responsibilities (controls customers must implement)

Additional FedRAMP package documents required:
  - System Security Plan (SSP) — FedRAMP template
  - SSP Attachments: Control Implementation Summary (CIS), Customer
    Responsibility Matrix (CRM), FIPS 199 categorization, system inventory,
    network diagrams, data flow diagrams, ports/protocols/services list
  - Security Assessment Plan (SAP) — developed by 3PAO
  - Security Assessment Report (SAR) — produced by 3PAO
  - Plan of Action and Milestones (POA&M) — maintained by CSP
  - FedRAMP Readiness Assessment Report (RAR) — optional but recommended

Action items:
  - Download the current FedRAMP SSP template from fedramp.gov
  - Map your existing ISO 27001 documentation (SoA, ISMS policies,
    risk assessment) to SSP sections — most will supplement rather than
    replace SSP narratives
  - Assign ISSO / compliance team to write control narratives for all
    325+ Moderate controls
  - Develop architecture diagrams and data flow diagrams at FedRAMP
    required detail level
  - Engage a FedRAMP consultant or use FedRAMP Marketplace CSP resources
    for SSP template guidance

SECTION 3 — RMF PROCESS FOR FEDRAMP (SP 800-37 Rev 2 ADAPTED)
================================================================================

FedRAMP is operationalized through the NIST Risk Management Framework
(SP 800-37 Rev 2), adapted with FedRAMP-specific requirements. There are two
authorization paths:

  AGENCY ATO PATH: CSP works directly with a specific federal agency customer.
    The agency Authorizing Official (AO) grants an Authority to Operate (ATO).
    Fastest for single-agency relationships.

  JAB P-ATO PATH: CSP works with the Joint Authorization Board (JAB) —
    comprised of DoD CIO, DHS CIO, and GSA CIO. Results in a Provisional ATO
    (P-ATO) accepted by all federal agencies. Highest rigor; longest timeline.
    JAB prioritizes CSPs using the FedRAMP Connect process.

Both paths follow the same seven RMF steps:

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
STEP 1 — PREPARE
SP 800-37 Rev 2, Chapter 2; FedRAMP: fedramp.gov/prepare/
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
Purpose: Establish the organizational and system context; assign roles;
         understand the authorization path.

Key tasks for FedRAMP Moderate:
  1. Assign FedRAMP roles:
     - Authorizing Official (AO): agency CISO or designated senior official
       (for Agency ATO) or JAB representatives (for P-ATO)
     - System Owner (SO): CSP executive accountable for the system
     - Information System Security Officer (ISSO): manages day-to-day
       security operations and SSP
     - FedRAMP Point of Contact (POC): CSP contact for FedRAMP PMO
  2. Understand FedRAMP boundary: define what is "in scope" for authorization
     (the Authorization Boundary — all components processing, transmitting,
     or storing federal data)
  3. Select authorization path (Agency ATO vs. JAB P-ATO)
  4. Conduct initial gap assessment against FedRAMP Moderate baseline
  5. Develop remediation roadmap and project plan
  6. Register system in FedRAMP Marketplace (fedramp.gov/marketplace)
  7. Engage 3PAO early

Key outputs: Assigned roles; selected authorization path; gap remediation plan;
             FedRAMP Marketplace registration.

Timeline estimate: 1–3 months

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
STEP 2 — CATEGORIZE
FIPS 199; SP 800-60; FedRAMP: FIPS 199 template
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
Purpose: Determine the system's security impact level (Low, Moderate, or High)
         based on the federal data it processes, stores, and transmits.

Key tasks for FedRAMP Moderate:
  1. Identify all federal information types processed using SP 800-60 Vol II
  2. Assess Confidentiality, Integrity, and Availability impact for each type
  3. Apply the high-water mark: overall system category = highest C/I/A value
  4. For FedRAMP Moderate: system must be categorized as FIPS 199 Moderate
     (at least one of C/I/A = Moderate; none = High)
  5. Complete and sign the FIPS 199 Security Categorization document
     (AO and SO signatures required)
  6. Incorporate categorization into SSP Section 2

Note: If categorization yields a High impact level for any objective, the
system requires the FedRAMP High baseline (~421 controls), not Moderate.

Key outputs: Signed FIPS 199 Security Categorization document; system
             description in SSP Sections 1–2.

Timeline estimate: 2–4 weeks

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
STEP 3 — SELECT
SP 800-53B (Moderate baseline); FedRAMP baseline documentation
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
Purpose: Select, tailor, and document the controls for the system.

Key tasks for FedRAMP Moderate:
  1. Start with SP 800-53B Moderate baseline (~325 controls/enhancements)
  2. Apply FedRAMP overlay: FedRAMP-specific ODV values override the standard
     baseline parameters (see Gap 6 above)
  3. Identify inherited controls:
     - Controls implemented by your underlying IaaS/PaaS provider
       (e.g., AWS GovCloud, Azure Government, Google Cloud) that are inherited
     - Document in the Control Implementation Summary (CIS) and SSP
     - Your IaaS/PaaS provider must have a FedRAMP authorization for
       you to inherit their controls
  4. Identify customer-responsible controls: controls the agency customer
     must implement (document in Customer Responsibility Matrix — CRM)
  5. Identify system-specific controls: controls you implement yourself
  6. Assign ODV parameter values for all controls with organization-defined
     parameters (use FedRAMP-required values where specified)
  7. Document tailoring decisions in SSP
  8. Develop Continuous Monitoring Strategy (CA-7 narrative)

Key outputs: Complete control selection documented in SSP; ConMon strategy;
             CIS; CRM.

Timeline estimate: 2–4 months (dependent on SSP writing resources)

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
STEP 4 — IMPLEMENT
SP 800-53 Rev 5 (implementation guidance); FedRAMP SSP template
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
Purpose: Implement all selected controls and document implementation in the SSP.

Key tasks for FedRAMP Moderate:
  1. Implement all system-specific controls (technical, operational,
     and management controls)
  2. Write SSP control narratives for all ~325 controls:
     - For each control: what is implemented, how it is implemented,
       who is responsible, what evidence exists
     - Address all control parameters (ODV values must be populated)
     - Distinguish base control from enhancements
  3. Complete SSP attachments:
     - System architecture diagrams (with authorization boundary marked)
     - Data flow diagrams (showing federal data flows)
     - Network diagrams (logical and physical)
     - Hardware/software inventory (CM-8)
     - Ports, protocols, services list
     - User types and privileges list
  4. Conduct internal pre-assessment / readiness review
  5. Complete POC for FedRAMP Readiness Assessment (optional but strongly
     recommended for first-time authorizations)

Key outputs: Completed SSP with all narratives; all SSP attachments;
             implemented controls with evidence artifacts.

Timeline estimate: 3–9 months (typically the longest phase for first ATO)

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
STEP 5 — ASSESS
SP 800-53A Rev 5; FedRAMP SAP/SAR templates; 3PAO-conducted
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
Purpose: Determine if controls are implemented correctly, operating as intended,
         and producing the desired outcome.

Key tasks for FedRAMP Moderate:
  1. 3PAO develops Security Assessment Plan (SAP) using SP 800-53A procedures
     and FedRAMP SAP template
  2. CSP reviews and approves SAP; agency AO concurs (for Agency ATO path)
  3. 3PAO conducts assessment using three methods:
     - EXAMINE: Review SSP, policies, procedures, configuration files,
       architecture diagrams, audit logs, training records
     - INTERVIEW: Discuss implementation with ISSO, system administrators,
       operators, developers, end users
     - TEST: Run vulnerability scans, penetration test, configuration checks,
       firewall rule reviews, backup restoration tests, access control tests
  4. 3PAO produces Security Assessment Report (SAR):
     - Executive summary with authorization recommendation
     - Control-by-control findings (Satisfied vs. Other Than Satisfied)
     - Risk ratings for OTS findings
     - Penetration test results
  5. CSP responds to SAR findings:
     - Remediate findings where possible before authorization
     - Open POA&M items for findings not yet remediated
  6. CSP develops initial POA&M from SAR OTS findings

FedRAMP-specific assessment requirements:
  - Annual penetration test using FedRAMP penetration test guidance
  - Automated vulnerability scanning results submitted to FedRAMP PMO
  - FedRAMP SAR template must be used
  - 3PAO independence must be maintained (no conflict of interest)

Key outputs: SAP; SAR; initial POA&M; 3PAO authorization recommendation.

Timeline estimate: 2–4 months (3PAO assessment engagement)

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
STEP 6 — AUTHORIZE
SP 800-37 Rev 2, Chapter 2.7; FedRAMP authorization process
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
Purpose: Senior official (Authorizing Official) formally accepts residual risk
         and grants or denies Authority to Operate.

Authorization package (submitted to AO or JAB):
  1. System Security Plan (SSP) — with all attachments
  2. Security Assessment Report (SAR) — produced by 3PAO
  3. Plan of Action and Milestones (POA&M) — with remediation timelines
  4. FedRAMP Authorization Package cover letter / executive summary

Agency ATO path:
  - CSP submits complete authorization package to agency AO
  - Agency reviews package (often with FedRAMP PMO support)
  - Agency AO makes authorization decision:
    * Authorization to Operate (ATO) — risk is acceptable; valid for 1 year
      (with ConMon) or up to 3 years
    * Denial of Authorization to Operate (DATO) — risk not acceptable;
      system must not process federal data until remediated
  - ATO letter issued; system listed as "FedRAMP Authorized" in Marketplace

JAB P-ATO path:
  - FedRAMP PMO conducts package review (kick-off through final package)
  - JAB technical reviewers assess the package
  - JAB issues Provisional ATO (P-ATO) if risk is acceptable
  - P-ATO can be leveraged by all federal agencies (reduces per-agency review)

Key FedRAMP authorization metrics (Moderate systems):
  - No unmitigated Critical or High risks at time of authorization
  - Open High POA&M items must have realistic remediation plans
  - AO accepts residual risk for all open POA&M items

Key outputs: ATO letter (Agency path) or P-ATO letter (JAB path);
             FedRAMP Marketplace listing updated to "Authorized."

Timeline estimate: 1–4 months (Agency ATO); 6–18 months (JAB P-ATO)

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
STEP 7 — MONITOR
SP 800-37 Rev 2, Chapter 2.8; SP 800-137; FedRAMP ConMon Guide
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
Purpose: Maintain ongoing situational awareness about the security posture;
         detect and respond to changes; sustain the ATO.

FedRAMP ConMon ongoing requirements:
  1. Monthly deliverables to FedRAMP PMO and agency customers:
     - Vulnerability scan results (OS, web app — monthly; DB — weekly)
     - Updated POA&M (with remediation status)
     - Updated system inventory
     - Updated CIS (Control Implementation Summary)
  2. Annual deliverables:
     - Annual assessment by 3PAO (all controls or a selected subset per
       FedRAMP ConMon Strategy guide)
     - Annual penetration test by 3PAO
     - Updated SSP (reflecting any significant changes)
     - Annual security review
  3. Significant Change Requests (SCRs):
     - Required before making significant changes to the authorization
       boundary, architecture, or security posture
     - CSP submits SCR to FedRAMP PMO; may trigger re-assessment
     - Examples: new cloud region, new external service, major architecture
       change, new data type
  4. Incident reporting:
     - US-CERT / CISA notification within 1 hour for major incidents
     - Agency customer notification per SLA / contract terms
     - Monthly operational reports to FedRAMP PMO
  5. Ongoing POA&M management:
     - Monthly updates required
     - Critical items: remediate within 30 days
     - High items: remediate within 90 days
     - Risk acceptances require ISSO, SO, and AO approval
  6. FedRAMP Marketplace status maintenance:
     - System remains "Authorized" only with current ConMon deliverables
     - FedRAMP PMO may revoke authorization for ConMon non-compliance

Continuous Authorization (preferred): Rather than periodic full re-authorization
every 3 years, FedRAMP encourages continuous authorization — the AO remains
continuously informed via ConMon deliverables and makes ongoing risk acceptance
decisions. This avoids the "big bang" re-authorization cycle.

Key outputs: Monthly ConMon deliverables; updated POA&M; annual 3PAO assessment;
             incident reports; SCRs as needed; sustained ATO status.
