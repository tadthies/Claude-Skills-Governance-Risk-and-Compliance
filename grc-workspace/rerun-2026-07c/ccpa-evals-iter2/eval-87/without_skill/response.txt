# Handling a Combined CCPA/CPRA Right to Know + Right to Delete Request

When a California resident submits both a **Right to Know** (access) request and a **Right to Delete** request, you need a coordinated process. Here's a step-by-step walkthrough.

## Step 1: Log and Acknowledge the Request

- Record the date received, the requestor's identity/contact info, and which rights are being invoked (know + delete).
- Confirm receipt within **10 business days**, describing your verification process and expected response timeline.
- You have **45 calendar days** from receipt to respond substantively (extendable once by another 45 days if reasonably necessary, with notice to the consumer explaining the delay).

## Step 2: Identity Verification

Verification must be reasonable and proportionate to the sensitivity of the data and the risk of harm from improper disclosure/deletion.

- **For a Right to Know (specific pieces of data) request**: Requires a higher standard of verification (e.g., matching 2-3 data points you already hold, such as name, email, account number, plus possibly a signed declaration under penalty of perjury) because disclosing specific personal information to the wrong person is high-risk.
- **For a Right to Delete request**: Also requires reasonable verification, though if you can't verify the requestor to the standard needed for deletion, you can treat it as a request to opt-out of sale/sharing instead (a lower bar), or ask for additional information.
- **If using a password-protected account**: You can verify through the existing authentication method (e.g., login + re-authentication), which is often sufficient.
- **If you cannot verify**: You may deny the request, but must explain why and, where feasible, offer the consumer another way to submit information for verification. Do not require the consumer to pay a fee or create an account they don't already have, just to submit a request.
- Document your verification decision and rationale for audit purposes.

## Step 3: Handle the Right to Know Request

1. **Search all systems** (CRM, marketing platforms, data warehouses, support tickets, analytics, backups if reasonably accessible, third-party processors acting as service providers) for personal information tied to the verified identity.
2. Compile a response that includes, for the preceding 12 months (or since collection if you offer more):
   - Categories of personal information collected
   - Categories of sources
   - Business/commercial purposes for collecting/selling/sharing
   - Categories of third parties with whom you've disclosed, sold, or shared PI
   - The **specific pieces of personal information** collected about that individual (this is the more detailed "right to know specific pieces" version, distinct from the general categories disclosure required in your privacy policy)
3. Deliver the response in a **portable, readily usable format** that allows the consumer to transmit the information to another entity without hindrance (commonly a downloadable file, e.g., CSV or PDF), or by mail if requested.
4. Do not disclose certain sensitive identifiers even in response to a verified request — e.g., **Social Security numbers, driver's license numbers, financial account numbers, health/medical IDs, account passwords/security Q&A** should generally be redacted or not disclosed in full even to the verified consumer, to avoid creating a security risk.

## Step 4: Handle the Right to Delete Request

1. Once identity is verified, delete personal information from your records **and direct service providers/contractors to delete it as well**, unless an exception applies.
2. **Common exceptions that allow you to retain data** (you may deny deletion, in whole or part, if the data is necessary to):
   - Complete the transaction, provide a good/service requested, or fulfill a warranty/product recall
   - Detect security incidents, protect against malicious/fraudulent/illegal activity, or prosecute those responsible
   - Debug to identify/repair errors that impair existing intended functionality
   - Exercise free speech, or another consumer's right to free speech
   - Comply with the California Electronic Communications Privacy Act
   - Engage in public/peer-reviewed scientific, historical, or statistical research in the public interest (with consent/de-identification safeguards)
   - Enable solely internal uses reasonably aligned with consumer expectations based on the relationship
   - Comply with a **legal obligation** (e.g., tax records, financial recordkeeping requirements)
   - Otherwise use the information internally, lawfully, in a manner compatible with the context in which it was provided
3. For any data retained under an exception, you should **document which exception applies** and, if practical, tell the consumer which categories were deleted vs. retained and why.
4. If you sold or shared the personal information with third parties, you must also **notify those third parties** to delete the data, unless doing so is impossible or involves disproportionate effort.

## Step 5: Order of Operations (Know vs. Delete)

Best practice: **process the Right to Know request first (or simultaneously), then process deletion.** If you delete first, you may destroy the very data needed to fulfill the access request. Many companies compile the "know" disclosure package, deliver or finalize it, and then execute deletion — or do both in parallel using a snapshot taken at verification time.

## Step 6: Respond to the Consumer

- Provide a **combined response letter/portal notification** addressing both requests: what was disclosed (attach or link to the data), and what was deleted vs. retained (with exception basis cited for retained data).
- Confirm deletion has been completed (or will be completed within the applicable timeframe) and that service providers/third parties were instructed accordingly.
- If you denied all or part of either request, explain why (e.g., failed verification, applicable exception) and inform them of their right to appeal or complain to the CPPA/Attorney General.

## Step 7: Recordkeeping

- Retain records of the request, verification steps, and actions taken for at least 24 months for compliance/audit purposes, but do not retain more personal information than needed for this purpose.

## Practical Tips
- Use a standardized intake form/workflow (web form, toll-free number, or both, as required) to reduce ad hoc handling.
- Train customer service staff to recognize and route these requests promptly — mishandled or ignored requests are a common enforcement trigger.
- If you operate a "right to know" and "right to delete" workflow through a Data Subject Request (DSR) management tool, make sure it covers all your data systems, not just your primary CRM.

**Disclaimer**: This is general guidance, not legal advice. If in doubt about verification standards or exception applicability for a specific request, consult California privacy counsel.
