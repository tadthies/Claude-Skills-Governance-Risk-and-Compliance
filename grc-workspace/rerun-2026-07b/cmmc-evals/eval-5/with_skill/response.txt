# Subcontractor CMMC Gaps — Your Flow-Down Obligations Under DFARS 252.204-7021

Discovering that subcontractors handling CUI have no CMMC certification is a serious exposure for you as the prime — DFARS 252.204-7021(c) places the flow-down obligation squarely on you, and it doesn't get waived just because the subcontractor is out of compliance. Here's what applies and what to do before the next option period.

## Your Flow-Down Obligations Under DFARS 252.204-7021(c)

As the prime contractor, you are required to:

1. **Include CMMC requirements in all subcontracts** where the subcontractor processes, stores, or transmits CUI — this is not optional or discretionary once CUI is involved.
2. **Specify the required CMMC level in the subcontract itself.** The level flows down based on what CUI the subcontractor actually handles and the criticality of the program, not automatically your own level.
3. **Verify subcontractor certification before award** — checking the subcontractor's status in SPRS and the CMMC-AB registry (cyberAB.us) is a precondition, not a formality. Award (or continuation) without verification is itself a compliance failure on your part.
4. **Flow this down through all tiers.** Sub-subcontractors are not exempt — if your subcontractor further subcontracts CUI-touching work, they must flow the same requirements down, and ultimately you are accountable for the integrity of that chain as the prime.

Because you've discovered the gap *after* the fact, you are currently out of compliance with your own prime contract obligations, not just carrying subcontractor risk. This needs to be treated as an active compliance gap in your supply chain security program, not a passive risk note.

## What "No CMMC Certification" Actually Means Here

Before deciding next steps, pin down precisely what's missing for each subcontractor:

- Do they have **no SPRS score submitted at all** (i.e., haven't even self-assessed), or do they have a self-assessment score but lack a required C3PAO certification for a critical program?
- What CMMC **level** should apply to each of them, based on the CUI they specifically handle (map CUI categories and flows to each subcontractor individually — the level isn't necessarily the same for both, and isn't necessarily the same as yours)?
- Is either subcontractor working on a "critical" program requiring C3PAO assessment, or would Level 2 self-assessment suffice for their scope?

## Recommended Action Plan Before the Next Option Period

**1. Formal notice and risk triage (immediate)**
- Notify both subcontractors in writing that their lack of certification is a contract compliance gap that must be resolved.
- Determine, for each, the CUI they handle and the CMMC level required for that scope (don't assume it matches your own level).

**2. Update or execute compliant subcontract language**
- Ensure current (or renewal) subcontract documents explicitly state the required CMMC level and reference DFARS 252.204-7021 flow-down language.
- If the existing subcontracts are silent or vague on this, that is itself a gap in your subcontract management process — fix the template going forward for all CUI-touching subcontractors, not just these two.

**3. Require a remediation path with a deadline**
- Ask each subcontractor for a gap assessment, a target self-assessment/SPRS submission date, or a C3PAO engagement timeline if their program is critical.
- Set a hard internal deadline tied to your option period decision point — if a subcontractor cannot demonstrate at least a good-faith SPRS self-assessment score and credible plan before the option period, that is a basis for reconsidering the award.

**4. Reassess award/continuation decisions**
- Before exercising the next option period, make a documented go/no-go decision for each subcontractor based on their compliance trajectory. Continuing to flow CUI to an uncertified subcontractor without a credible remediation plan compounds your own risk exposure as the prime.
- Consider interim risk mitigations if you must continue the relationship short-term: restrict the CUI actually shared, require additional contractual security commitments, or increase oversight/audit rights until certification is achieved.

**5. Document everything in your supply chain security program**
- Record the CUI-to-subcontractor mapping, the level determination for each, the verification steps taken (or gap identified), and the remediation plan with dates. This documentation is exactly what a DCMA/DIBCAC or C3PAO review would expect to see demonstrating active supply chain risk management, and it materially reduces your own liability exposure if this surfaces during your own assessment or a DoD audit.

**6. Self-assess your own exposure**
- Since DFARS 252.204-7021 obligations run through you, consult with your contracts/legal team on whether this gap needs to be disclosed to your contracting officer, particularly if CUI has already been shared with these subcontractors without verified certification. False or incomplete affirmations carry False Claims Act exposure — silence is not a safe default here.

## Bottom Line

You cannot simply flow the requirement down and consider your obligation discharged — DFARS 252.204-7021(c) requires you to **verify** certification before award/continuation, not merely reference it in contract language. The immediate priority is: (1) map exactly what CUI and what level applies to each subcontractor, (2) get formal remediation commitments with dates before your option period decision, and (3) loop in contracts/legal on disclosure obligations given that CUI may have already been shared without verified compliance.

---
*This is general compliance guidance, not legal advice. Given the potential contractual and False Claims Act exposure here, engage your contracts counsel before making award/continuation decisions or making any disclosures to your contracting officer.*
