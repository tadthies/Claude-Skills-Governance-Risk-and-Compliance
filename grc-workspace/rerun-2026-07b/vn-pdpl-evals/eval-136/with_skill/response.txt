## VN-PDPL Gap Analysis — Singapore-Based SaaS Company (Vietnam Customers)

### Executive Summary

Your company processes personal data of Vietnamese data subjects (names, emails, payment data) even though you are headquartered in Singapore and store data in Singapore. Vietnam's Law on Personal Data Protection No. 91/2025/QH15 (effective 1 January 2026) has **extraterritorial reach**: it applies to any foreign organisation processing the personal data of Vietnamese individuals, regardless of where the processing or storage occurs. As of today (3 July 2026), the law has been in force for six months, so transitional grace periods for general compliance have lapsed — only the time-bound SME exemptions (Articles 21, 22, 33(2)) remain available, and only if you qualify as an SME.

You have five material gaps: (1) invalid consent mechanism (pre-ticked checkbox), (2) no DPO, (3) no impact assessment of any kind (domestic DPIA or cross-border transfer), (4) sensitive personal data (payment/financial data) being processed without the heightened consent required for sensitive categories, and (5) an undocumented cross-border transfer (Vietnam → Singapore) that appears never to have been assessed. These are foundational gaps that touch consent validity, cross-border transfer legality, and governance structure — they should be treated as high priority.

### Priority Actions

1. **Immediate (0–2 weeks):** Fix the consent mechanism — remove the pre-ticked checkbox and replace with valid opt-in consent (Decree 356 Article 6). Freeze/pause new sensitive-data (payment) collection until separate explicit consent is implemented, or implement it urgently in parallel.
2. **Immediate (0–4 weeks):** Prepare and submit the overdue cross-border transfer impact assessment to the Ministry of Public Security; this is your highest financial-penalty exposure (up to 5% of Vietnam revenue).
3. **Short term (0–6 weeks):** Determine SME/micro-enterprise status to confirm whether Article 21 (DPIA) and Article 22 (security measures) exemptions apply; if not exempt, or if processing sensitive data at scale disqualifies you from the exemption, file the domestic DPIA.
4. **Short term (2–8 weeks):** Appoint a DPO (internal or outsourced) and publish contact details.
5. **Short term (4–8 weeks):** Update the privacy notice and build/document the data subject rights fulfilment workflow with required timeframes.
6. **Ongoing:** Establish a breach register and 72-hour notification procedure; conduct security control review of Singapore data storage (encryption at rest/in transit, access controls) consistent with Decree 356 cloud/security expectations.

### SME Exemptions Applicable (if any)

The SME exemption (Articles 21, 22, 33(2)) allows small and micro enterprises to opt out of the DPIA and certain security/processor obligations for 5 years from 1 January 2026 — **but only if the company does not process sensitive personal data or process at large scale.** Because this company processes payment data (a sensitive category under Decree 356 Article 4(7)) as a core part of its SaaS offering, it likely **does not qualify** for the DPIA exemption even if it otherwise meets small/micro enterprise size thresholds. Company size and headcount/revenue should be confirmed against official SME criteria, but the sensitive-data disqualifier is the more decisive factor here. The DPO requirement is also likely to apply regardless of size, given sensitive data processing.

*This analysis is general compliance guidance, not legal advice. Given the overdue cross-border filing and consent validity issues, engage qualified Vietnamese counsel or an accredited assessor promptly to confirm exposure and remediation timelines.*
