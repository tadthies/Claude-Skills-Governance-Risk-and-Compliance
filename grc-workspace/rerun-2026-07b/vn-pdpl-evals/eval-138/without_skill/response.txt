# Does Vietnam's PDPL Apply to a 15-Person Hanoi Startup? Small Business Exemptions

## Short Answer
There is no blanket "small business exemption" from Vietnam's Personal Data Protection Law (PDPL) itself — the law applies based on what data you process and how, not primarily on headcount. However, the *intensity* of certain obligations (particularly the formal Data Protection Impact Assessment and the requirement for a dedicated DPO/department) does scale with the volume, sensitivity, and risk profile of your data processing, and Vietnam's regime (building on the Decree 13 framework it supersedes/extends) has historically included some lighter-touch treatment for micro/small enterprises with limited, low-risk processing.

## What This Means for a 15-Person Startup

### 1. Core Obligations Likely Still Apply
Regardless of size, if you collect and process personal data of individuals (employees, customers, users), baseline obligations generally apply:
- Having a lawful basis (typically consent) for processing
- Providing a privacy notice/policy explaining what you collect and why
- Honoring data subject rights (access, correction, deletion, withdrawal of consent)
- Implementing reasonable security safeguards
- Maintaining basic records of processing activities

These are not typically waived for small companies — a 15-person startup handling customer data is still a "data controller" under the law.

### 2. Data Protection Impact Assessment (DPIA)
- The DPIA requirement is generally tied to the *nature and risk* of processing rather than a strict headcount threshold. If you process personal data at meaningful scale, process sensitive personal data (health, biometric, financial, children's data, location, etc.), or your processing could pose material risk to data subjects, a DPIA is likely required regardless of your company size.
- If your startup's processing is genuinely limited (e.g., basic contact info for a small user base, no sensitive categories, low volume), you may fall into a lower-risk tier where a lightweight or streamlined assessment suffices — but "we're small" alone is not a formal exemption; it's the *nature of the data and processing* that drives the requirement.
- **Practical read:** Even if you believe you're exempt, it's prudent to conduct at least a lightweight internal assessment and document your reasoning — this itself is evidence of good-faith compliance if questioned by regulators.

### 3. Data Protection Officer (DPO)
- The requirement to appoint a DPO (or designate a responsible data protection function) is generally linked to factors like:
  - Volume of personal data processed
  - Whether sensitive personal data is processed
  - Whether processing is a core/significant part of your business activities (e.g., a data-driven platform vs. incidental HR data)
- A 15-person startup with limited, low-risk data processing (e.g., typical SaaS with a small user base, no sensitive data categories) may not need a full-time dedicated DPO, but should still designate a responsible individual (even part-time, e.g., the founder or ops lead) as the point of accountability for data protection matters. This is a much lower bar than hiring a dedicated compliance officer.
- If your startup processes sensitive personal data (e.g., health, financial/payment, biometric data) or scales to a large user base, the DPO requirement becomes more likely to formally apply regardless of your 15-person headcount.

### 4. Registration/Notification Requirements
- Some processing activities (particularly cross-border transfers or processing of sensitive personal data) may trigger dossier/documentation requirements with the Ministry of Public Security regardless of company size — size affects practical enforcement priority, not legal applicability.

## Bottom Line for Your Startup
- **No automatic size-based exemption** from the PDPL's core obligations (consent, notice, data subject rights, security).
- **Risk-based, not headcount-based**, is the operative principle for DPIA and DPO requirements — assess based on what data you handle, not how many employees you have.
- **Practical starting point for a 15-person startup:**
  1. Map what personal data you actually collect and why (data inventory).
  2. Identify whether you handle any sensitive categories (health, financial, biometric, children's data).
  3. Fix consent/notice basics regardless of size (these are almost never exempted).
  4. Designate a responsible person for data protection (doesn't need to be a full-time hire).
  5. Do a lightweight documented risk assessment even if you conclude a full DPIA isn't required — this protects you if challenged.
  6. Revisit thresholds as you scale — obligations that don't strictly apply today may apply as your user base or data sensitivity grows.

## Important Caveat
The precise thresholds, exemption criteria, and any explicit small/micro-enterprise carve-outs are defined in the PDPL's implementing decree and related guidance from the Ministry of Public Security, and these details are subject to official interpretation and potential updates. Given this is a newly effective law (2026), confirm current exact thresholds with Vietnamese legal counsel rather than relying solely on general principles.
