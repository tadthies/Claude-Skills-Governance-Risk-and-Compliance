# Withdrawal of Consent Under Vietnam's PDPL — Process, Timeframes, and Consequences

## Right to Withdraw Consent
Vietnam's Personal Data Protection Law and its implementing decree recognize withdrawal of consent as a core data subject right. The general principle — consistent with how Vietnam's framework has evolved from Decree 13/2023 into the PDPL — is that **withdrawal must be as easy as giving consent** in the first place.

## Process

### 1. How the Request Can Be Made
- The data subject should be able to withdraw consent through a simple, accessible channel — ideally the same channel used to give consent (e.g., an in-app toggle, an account settings page, a clear "unsubscribe"/"withdraw consent" link, or a written/email request to your designated contact or DPO).
- You should not require the customer to jump through disproportionate hoops (e.g., mailing a physical letter) if consent was originally given with one click.

### 2. What the Business Must Do Upon Receiving a Withdrawal Request
1. **Acknowledge the request** promptly.
2. **Verify the requester's identity** (proportionate to risk — you don't want to over-collect data just to process a withdrawal, but you need reasonable assurance it's a legitimate request from the actual data subject).
3. **Cease the specific processing activity** tied to that consent going forward. Withdrawal is not retroactive — it does not invalidate processing that occurred lawfully before withdrawal, but it does require you to stop new/continued processing based on that consent basis.
4. **Assess whether another lawful basis applies** for any processing you need to continue (e.g., contractual necessity for order fulfillment, legal obligation for tax/accounting records). If no other lawful basis exists, you must stop that processing.
5. **Propagate the withdrawal** across systems and to any third parties/processors you've shared the data with for that purpose, where feasible.
6. **Log the withdrawal** (date, scope, action taken) for your own compliance records.

## Timeframes
- The implementing decree generally expects businesses to act on a withdrawal request **without undue delay** — commonly benchmarked in practice against a short window (organizations frequently target no more than a few business days to a similar short period, consistent with how Decree 13-based practice has been applied, though the PDPL's implementing decree may specify a more precise deadline).
- If immediate technical cessation isn't possible (e.g., data embedded in scheduled marketing batches), the business should stop as soon as operationally feasible and communicate this to the data subject.
- **Recommendation:** Adopt an internal SLA (e.g., process and confirm within 5–10 business days) even if the precise statutory number isn't crystal clear from public guidance, since regulators generally judge "undue delay" against what is reasonably achievable, not against a business's own scheduling convenience.

## Consequences of Withdrawal

### For the Data Subject
- The business must stop processing personal data for the purpose(s) tied to the withdrawn consent.
- If certain services depend on that consent (e.g., personalized recommendations, marketing communications), the customer should be clearly informed that withdrawal means those specific features/communications will stop — but core contractual services (e.g., completing an already-placed order) generally continue if based on a separate lawful basis (contract performance), not consent.
- The customer retains other data subject rights independently (e.g., right to request deletion, which is a distinct right from withdrawal of consent and may need to be exercised separately if they want data erased entirely).

### For the Business
- Must cease the specific processing activity — continuing to process data after a valid withdrawal (without another lawful basis) is a compliance violation and exposes the company to regulatory risk (potential sanctions from the Ministry of Public Security) and reputational/customer trust risk.
- Should NOT penalize the customer for withdrawing (e.g., cannot degrade unrelated service quality or restrict access to services not actually dependent on that consent).
- Should retain a record that consent was withdrawn (and when) — ironically, this record itself may need to be kept (e.g., under a "suppression list" concept) precisely so the business doesn't inadvertently re-process or re-contact the individual for the withdrawn purpose.
- If data was shared with third parties/processors under that consent, the business is generally expected to notify those parties of the withdrawal so they can also cease relevant processing.

## Practical Checklist for Handling a Withdrawal Request
- [ ] Provide an easy, obvious withdrawal channel (matching the ease of the original consent)
- [ ] Acknowledge receipt of the request promptly
- [ ] Verify identity proportionately
- [ ] Determine scope: which specific processing purpose(s) does the withdrawal cover?
- [ ] Check for an alternate lawful basis for any processing you wish to continue
- [ ] Stop the relevant processing without undue delay (target a short, defined internal SLA)
- [ ] Notify relevant third parties/processors of the withdrawal
- [ ] Log the withdrawal and confirm completion to the customer
- [ ] Clearly communicate any resulting service changes to the customer

## Important Caveat
The PDPL's implementing decree may specify a precise statutory deadline for acting on withdrawal requests and more detailed procedural requirements than summarized here. Given this is a newly effective legal framework (as of 2026), confirm the exact timeframe and any prescribed procedural form with Vietnamese legal counsel before finalizing your internal withdrawal-handling process.
