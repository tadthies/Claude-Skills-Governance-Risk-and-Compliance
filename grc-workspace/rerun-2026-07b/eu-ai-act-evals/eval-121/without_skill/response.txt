# CV Screening Tool — EU AI Act Classification

## Is it high-risk?

Yes. An AI system used to rank or screen job applicants is very likely classified as **high-risk** under the EU AI Act (Regulation (EU) 2024/1689).

## Which Annex III area applies?

**Annex III, point 4 — Employment, workers' management and access to self-employment**, specifically the sub-category covering AI systems intended to be used for **recruitment or selection of natural persons**, including:
- Placing targeted job advertisements
- Analysing and filtering job applications
- **Evaluating candidates** (which covers ranking/scoring applicants)

A CV screening tool that ranks applicants falls squarely within this category because it is used to evaluate candidates and influence recruitment decisions.

## Compliance deadline

High-risk AI system obligations under Annex III generally apply from **2 August 2026**, which is the main high-risk system application date under the Act's staggered timeline (the Act entered into force 1 August 2024; prohibitions applied from 2 February 2025; GPAI obligations from 2 August 2025; most Annex III high-risk obligations from 2 August 2026; Annex I high-risk product-safety obligations from 2 August 2027).

Since today's date is July 3, 2026, this deadline is approaching in about one month — compliance work should already be well underway or complete.

## Main obligations as the provider

As the **provider** of a high-risk AI system, your main obligations include:

1. **Risk management system** — establish and maintain a continuous risk management process across the system's lifecycle (Article 9).
2. **Data governance** — ensure training, validation, and testing datasets are relevant, representative, and checked for biases, particularly bias against protected groups given the employment context (Article 10).
3. **Technical documentation** — prepare and maintain detailed technical documentation before the system is placed on the market (Article 11, Annex IV).
4. **Record-keeping / logging** — design the system to automatically log events over its lifetime to enable traceability (Article 12).
5. **Transparency and instructions for use** — provide clear information to deployers (employers) enabling them to interpret outputs and use the system correctly (Article 13).
6. **Human oversight** — design the system so it can be effectively overseen by natural persons, including the ability to override or disregard outputs (Article 14). This is critical for hiring decisions.
7. **Accuracy, robustness, and cybersecurity** — ensure appropriate levels of these throughout the lifecycle (Article 15).
8. **Conformity assessment** — carry out a conformity assessment (for most Annex III systems, this can be an internal/provider self-assessment against Annex VI, unless a harmonised standard requires third-party involvement) before placing the system on the market.
9. **CE marking and EU declaration of conformity** — affix CE marking and draw up a declaration of conformity (Articles 47, 48).
10. **Registration** — register the system in the EU high-risk AI database before placing it on the market (Article 49).
11. **Post-market monitoring** — establish a system to collect and review experience from use (Article 72).
12. **Incident reporting** — report serious incidents to market surveillance authorities (Article 73).
13. **Fundamental rights impact assessment support** — while this obligation formally falls on certain deployers (e.g., public bodies, or private entities providing public services, and in some readings extended to employers), as the provider you must supply the deployer with the information needed to complete it, and cooperate with deployers who must conduct one (Article 27).

## Additional practical notes

- If your customers (the employers) are deploying the tool, they are the **deployers** and have their own obligations (e.g., human oversight in practice, informing candidates/workers that an AI system is being used to make or assist decisions affecting them — this ties into worker information/consultation obligations and existing EU/national labor law, plus GDPR Article 22 on automated decision-making).
- Given the employment context, non-discrimination and bias testing should be a top priority, as biased CV screening is one of the most litigated AI risk areas globally.
- You should also assess whether a **Quality Management System (QMS)** is required (Article 17) — it is, for high-risk AI providers.
