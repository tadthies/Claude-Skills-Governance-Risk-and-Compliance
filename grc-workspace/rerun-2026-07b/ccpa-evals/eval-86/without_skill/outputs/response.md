# Are You Subject to CCPA/CPRA?

## Applicability Analysis

The CCPA, as amended by the CPRA, applies to a "business" — a for-profit entity that does business in California, determines the purposes and means of processing consumers' personal information, and meets **at least one** of three thresholds:

1. **Annual gross revenue** exceeding $25,000,000 (adjusted periodically for inflation) in the preceding calendar year; **or**
2. **Buys, sells, or shares** the personal information of **100,000 or more** California consumers or households annually; **or**
3. **Derives 50% or more of annual revenue** from selling or sharing California consumers' personal information.

**Your situation:** With $30M in annual revenue, you exceed the $25M revenue threshold (Threshold 1). This alone makes you a "business" subject to CCPA/CPRA — it does not matter that you only collect data from 80,000 California customers (below the 100,000 threshold for Criterion 2). Revenue alone is sufficient to trigger coverage.

**Conclusion: Yes, you are subject to CCPA/CPRA.**

## Core Obligations

### 1. Consumer Rights You Must Honor
- **Right to Know** — what personal information is collected, used, disclosed, sold, or shared, and the specific pieces of PI held about a consumer.
- **Right to Delete** — subject to enumerated exceptions (e.g., completing a transaction, legal compliance, security purposes).
- **Right to Correct** — inaccurate personal information.
- **Right to Opt-Out** of sale/sharing of personal information (including cross-context behavioral advertising, which counts as "sharing").
- **Right to Limit Use of Sensitive Personal Information** (SPI) for purposes beyond what's necessary to provide goods/services.
- **Right to Non-Discrimination** for exercising any of these rights.
- **Right to Data Portability**.

### 2. Notice Obligations
- **Notice at Collection** — provided at or before the point personal information is collected, listing categories of PI collected and purposes.
- **Privacy Policy** — a comprehensive, publicly posted policy updated at least every 12 months, describing categories of PI collected/sold/shared/disclosed in the preceding 12 months, consumer rights, and how to exercise them.
- **"Do Not Sell or Share My Personal Information"** link (or an opt-out preference signal mechanism, such as honoring the Global Privacy Control) if you sell or share PI.
- **"Limit the Use of My Sensitive Personal Information"** link if applicable.

### 3. Operational Requirements
- Establish **at least two methods** for consumers to submit requests to know/delete (e.g., toll-free number and a web form/email).
- **Identity verification** procedures reasonably designed to detect fraudulent requests without over-collecting data.
- Respond to verifiable consumer requests within **45 days** (extendable once by another 45 days with notice).
- **Employee training** for anyone handling consumer inquiries about the company's privacy practices or CCPA compliance.
- **Data inventory/mapping** to know what PI you hold, where it flows, and to whom it's disclosed.
- **Recordkeeping** — maintain records of consumer requests and responses for 24 months.

### 4. Vendor/Third-Party Management
- If you share PI with service providers, contractors, or third parties, you need **CCPA-compliant contracts** with required data-use restrictions and limitations (no use outside the specified business purpose, no combining data across clients, flow-down of consumer rights obligations, audit rights).
- If any data flows constitute a "sale" or "sharing" (e.g., ad tech, analytics with cross-context behavioral advertising), you must honor opt-outs and disclose this in your notices.

### 5. Security Obligations
- Implement **reasonable security procedures and practices** appropriate to the nature of the information, to protect PI from unauthorized access, destruction, use, modification, or disclosure.
- Note: a data breach resulting from failure to implement reasonable security can trigger a **private right of action** with statutory damages ($100–$750 per consumer per incident), even without proof of actual harm.

### 6. Risk Assessments & Cybersecurity Audits (CPRA additions, phased in via regulations)
- Businesses whose processing presents significant risk to consumer privacy or security may need to conduct **annual cybersecurity audits** and submit **risk assessments** to the California Privacy Protection Agency (CPPA), particularly if engaging in significant processing of sensitive PI or automated decision-making. Confirm current applicability thresholds and effective dates under the CPPA's finalized regulations, as these have been phased in over time.

### 7. Automated Decision-Making Technology (ADMT)
- If you use automated decision-making for significant decisions (e.g., pricing personalization based on profiling), CPRA regulations impose **pre-use notice and opt-out/access rights** — worth evaluating if you use any profiling or algorithmic personalization for your e-commerce customers.

## Practical Next Steps
1. Conduct a **data mapping exercise** to identify all PI collected, sources, purposes, recipients, and retention periods.
2. Determine if any data sharing (ad tech, analytics, marketing pixels) qualifies as a "sale" or "sharing" under CCPA's broad definitions — this is common for e-commerce sites running third-party ad/analytics tags.
3. Update your **privacy policy** and implement **notice-at-collection** banners/links.
4. Stand up **request intake mechanisms** (web form + toll-free number or email) and an internal workflow for identity verification, fulfillment, and tracking within the 45-day window.
5. Audit and update **vendor contracts** to include CCPA-mandated terms.
6. Implement a **"Do Not Sell or Share"** mechanism and honor **Global Privacy Control** signals.
7. Review security controls against a reasonableness standard (e.g., encryption, access controls, MFA) given the private right of action exposure for breaches.

*Note: This is general guidance based on the CCPA/CPRA statutory framework. Given the evolving CPPA regulations (especially around risk assessments, cybersecurity audits, and ADMT), you should confirm current thresholds and effective dates and consult counsel for a tailored compliance program.*
