# Handling a Combined Right to Know + Right to Delete Request

When a California resident submits both a **Request to Know** (specific pieces of personal information) and a **Request to Delete**, treat them as two distinct rights that must each be processed correctly, though they can move through your intake pipeline together.

## Step 1: Log and Acknowledge the Request

- Record the date received, the requestor's contact information, and which rights are being exercised (Know — specific pieces — and Delete).
- Confirm receipt within **10 business days**, describing your verification process and expected timeline.
- You have **45 calendar days** from receipt to substantively respond (extendable once by an additional 45 days if reasonably necessary, with notice to the consumer explaining the delay).

## Step 2: Identity Verification

Because this is a request for **specific pieces of personal information** (not just categories) and a deletion request, this is considered a higher-risk request requiring a **reasonably high degree of certainty** that the requestor is who they claim to be.

- **Match against existing account data**: if the consumer has an account with you, the most reliable method is authentication through existing login credentials (password + MFA if available).
- **If no account exists**, use a verification process proportionate to the sensitivity of the data — e.g., matching at least two or three data points you already hold (name, email, address, order history, last four digits of payment method) against the request.
- Do **not** collect new categories of personal information solely for verification unless absolutely necessary; if you must, that information should be used only for verification and deleted afterward.
- If you cannot verify identity to a reasonable degree of certainty:
  - For the **Request to Know (specific pieces)**: deny the specific-pieces portion, but you may still provide a response in **categories** (which requires a lower verification bar), or treat it as a non-verifiable request and explain why you can't fulfill it.
  - For the **Request to Delete**: you may deny it if identity cannot be verified, but you should still offer the consumer the opt-out/do-not-sell right, which does not require verification.
- Use an **authorized agent** check if someone other than the consumer submitted the request — you can require proof of authorization (signed permission) and may still independently verify the consumer directly.

## Step 3: Locate All Personal Information

- Search across all systems: CRM, marketing platforms, order management, support tickets, analytics, backups, and any third parties/service providers/contractors who process data on your behalf.
- Compile the specific pieces of PI collected in the past 12 months (or since your applicable lookback period), including inferences drawn about the consumer.

## Step 4: Respond to the Right to Know

Provide, in a **readily usable format** that allows the consumer to transmit the information to another entity without hindrance (e.g., portable, structured format such as CSV or PDF), and in a **secure manner** (e.g., through a secure account portal or encrypted transmission — never a bare unencrypted email with sensitive PI):

- The specific pieces of personal information collected.
- Categories of sources.
- Business/commercial purposes for collecting/selling/sharing.
- Categories of third parties with whom it was disclosed, sold, or shared.

**Exceptions/limits to disclosure of specific pieces:**
- You are not required to disclose certain sensitive identifiers even in a verified specific-pieces request — e.g., Social Security numbers, driver's license numbers, financial account numbers, health/genetic data, or account passwords/security Q&A — where disclosure would create a substantial, articulable privacy or security risk. Instead, you may note the category was collected without disclosing the specific value.
- Trade secret information or information that would require disclosing another individual's personal information can also be withheld/redacted.

## Step 5: Process the Right to Delete

Delete the personal information from your records **and** direct any service providers, contractors, and third parties who received the data to do the same, unless a statutory exception applies.

**Common exceptions that let you retain data (in whole or part) instead of deleting:**
1. **Complete the transaction** for which the PI was collected, provide a good/service requested, or perform a contract.
2. **Security purposes** — detect security incidents, protect against fraud/illegal activity, or ensure security/integrity of systems.
3. **Debug** to identify/repair errors impairing functionality.
4. **Free speech / other legal rights** exercised by you or another consumer.
5. **CalECPA compliance** (California Electronic Communications Privacy Act).
6. **Public interest research**, if conducted ethically and consistent with consumer expectations.
7. **Internal uses reasonably aligned with consumer expectations** based on the relationship.
8. **Legal compliance** — comply with a legal obligation (e.g., tax/accounting recordkeeping, statute of limitations for potential litigation).
9. **Internal lawful use compatible with the context** in which the PI was provided.

If an exception applies to only some data (e.g., you must retain transaction/tax records but can delete marketing profile data), apply **partial deletion** — delete what you can and retain only what's necessary under the exception, documenting your rationale.

**Deletion methods** — you may choose to permanently and completely erase the PI, de-identify it, or aggregate it, provided it can no longer reasonably identify the consumer.

## Step 6: Notify Downstream Recipients

- Instruct all **service providers and contractors** to delete the consumer's PI from their records.
- If you **sold or shared** the PI with third parties, notify them of the deletion request and direct them to delete it as well, unless doing so is impossible or involves disproportionate effort — document your outreach either way.

## Step 7: Document and Respond

- Send a written response confirming: (a) what specific-pieces information was disclosed (or why it was denied/redacted), and (b) confirmation of deletion, including which categories were deleted vs. retained under an exception and why.
- Keep records of the request and your response for **24 months** to demonstrate compliance in case of a CPPA or AG inquiry.
- Do not discriminate against the consumer (e.g., degraded service, price changes) for exercising these rights.

## Practical Tips
- If both requests arrive together, verify once, then fulfill both — but track them as separate rights in your compliance log since they have different disclosure standards and exception frameworks.
- Train your support/privacy team on the distinction between "categories" vs. "specific pieces" disclosure, since specific pieces requires higher verification and carries more redaction exceptions.
- Build a standard operating procedure so verification, search, and response steps are repeatable, auditable, and completed within the 45-day (or extended 90-day) window.

*This is general compliance guidance based on the CCPA/CPRA statutory and regulatory framework; verify current CPPA regulatory specifics and consult counsel for request-specific edge cases.*
