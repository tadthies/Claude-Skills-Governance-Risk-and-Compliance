# Double Materiality Under CSRD: Concept, Comparison, and Assessment Process

---

## 1. What Is Double Materiality?

Double materiality is the **foundational principle of CSRD sustainability reporting** (Directive (EU) 2022/2464, Art. 19a). It requires companies to assess and report on sustainability matters from two distinct and equally valid perspectives simultaneously:

**Perspective 1 — Impact Materiality ("Inside-Out")**
Does the company have actual or potential impacts on people or the environment, whether positive or negative? This perspective looks outward from the company into the world — how the company's activities, decisions, and value chain affect ecosystems, communities, workers, and society.

**Perspective 2 — Financial Materiality ("Outside-In")**
Does a sustainability matter generate — or could it generate — risks or opportunities that affect the company's financial position, financial performance, cash flows, access to finance, or cost of capital? This perspective looks inward — how external sustainability developments affect the company's own economic value.

**The critical rule (ESRS 1, para. 47):** A topic is material if it meets **either** the impact materiality threshold **or** the financial materiality threshold. Topics meeting both thresholds are doubly material. The two perspectives are assessed independently — a topic can be impact-material without being financially material, and vice versa, and both require full disclosure under the relevant ESRS standard.

This "OR" logic is what gives double materiality its breadth. A company cannot avoid reporting on a topic simply because it does not create financial risk — if the company causes significant harm to communities or the environment, that impact must be reported regardless of whether it affects the income statement.

## 2. How Double Materiality Differs from GRI and TCFD

### Side-by-Side Comparison

| Aspect | CSRD / ESRS | GRI Standards | TCFD |
|--------|-------------|---------------|------|
| **Materiality concept** | Double materiality — both impact AND financial perspectives required | Impact materiality — focused on company's effects on the world | Financial materiality — focused on climate risks/opportunities affecting the company |
| **Primary perspective** | Both inside-out and outside-in | Inside-out (impact on people/environment) | Outside-in (financial risk to company) |
| **Trigger for reporting** | Either perspective suffices (OR logic) | Significant impact on economy, environment, or people | Material financial risk or opportunity from climate |
| **Mandatory or voluntary** | Mandatory EU law | Voluntary global standard | Voluntary (mandatory in some jurisdictions, e.g., UK, NZ) |
| **Scope of sustainability topics** | All ESRS topics (E, S, G) across value chain | Company-determined based on stakeholder engagement | Climate-focused (physical and transition risks) |
| **Assurance required** | Yes — limited assurance from first reporting year (Art. 26a) | Optional | Optional |
| **Stakeholder engagement** | Mandatory input into DMA process | Central — stakeholder identification drives topic selection | Recommended but not prescribed |
| **Interoperability** | ESRS 1 Appendix C maps to GRI; ESRS E1 incorporates TCFD | ESRS references GRI — GRI reporters can identify gaps | TCFD reporters have a strong foundation for ESRS E1 |

### GRI: Impact Materiality Only

GRI's materiality concept (as defined in GRI 1: Foundation 2021) focuses on the company's **significant impacts on the economy, environment, and people** — essentially the inside-out perspective. GRI asks: what does the company do to the world? It does not require a systematic assessment of how sustainability matters financially affect the company. Stakeholder engagement is central to identifying material topics under GRI, but the lens is outward.

If a company uses GRI as its sole reporting framework, it may be capturing impact materiality in reasonable depth, but it will not have systematically assessed financial risks and opportunities from sustainability matters — the outside-in dimension that CSRD requires.

### TCFD: Financial Materiality Only

TCFD (Task Force on Climate-related Financial Disclosures) operates exclusively on **financial materiality** — specifically the outside-in view of climate risk. TCFD asks how physical risks (acute events like floods, chronic effects like sea-level rise) and transition risks (policy, technology, market, reputational) could affect the company's finances. It does not ask whether the company's activities are causing climate harm.

A company reporting under TCFD may have robust climate risk scenario analysis but no assessment of whether its own emissions are causing significant environmental impact — a key impact materiality question under ESRS E1.

### The CSRD Difference

CSRD requires **both** frameworks' logic applied across **all** sustainability topics, not just climate. A company must ask both:
- "What harm or benefit is our business causing to people and the planet?" (impact lens), and
- "What sustainability developments out there could affect our financial health?" (financial lens)

Neither lens can be skipped, and the answer to either is sufficient to trigger full disclosure obligations.

## 3. Conducting a Double Materiality Assessment: Step-by-Step Process

The DMA is the mandatory first step in CSRD compliance (ESRS 1, paras. 19–56 and 45–56). It must be completed **before** deciding which ESRS topical standards apply — it cannot be done retrospectively to justify pre-selected topics.

### Step 1: Understand the Business Context (ESRS 1, para. 45)

Before assessing materiality, the company must establish a clear picture of what it is assessing. This involves:

- **Mapping the business model:** What products and services does the company provide? In which markets and geographies does it operate?
- **Mapping the value chain:** Who are the key upstream suppliers (tier 1 and beyond)? What are the downstream channels, customers, and end-users? Where does the company have influence?
- **Identifying sector-specific risks:** Use sector references — SASB standards, GRI sector standards, ESRS sector standards (when published) — to establish a starting universe of relevant sustainability topics.

Output: A business context document and value chain map that scopes what the DMA must cover. Note that CSRD requires companies to consider both their own operations and their upstream and downstream value chain (ESRS 1, para. 46).

---

### Step 2: Identify Actual and Potential Impacts (ESRS 1, paras. 46–48)

Generate a long list of actual and potential impacts across all ESRS topic areas. Sources for identification include:

- Internal: operational reviews, incident and near-miss data, management interviews, procurement data
- External: stakeholder engagement, NGO and civil society reports, industry benchmarks, ESG ratings, peer company disclosures

**Stakeholder engagement is mandatory (ESRS 1, para. 22).** The company must engage meaningfully with affected stakeholders — not just investors. Required engagement includes workers (own workforce and value chain), local communities, customers and consumers, civil society, and regulators. Investor-only consultation is insufficient and would be flagged during assurance.

At this stage, classify each identified impact as:
- Actual (currently occurring) or Potential (could occur in future)
- Positive or Negative
- In own operations or in the value chain
- Short-term, medium-term, or long-term

Output: A long-list impact register covering all ESRS topic areas.

---

### Step 3: Assess the Significance of Impacts (Impact Materiality Scoring)

Rate each identified impact using the ESRS significance formula:

- **Negative actual impacts:** Scale × Scope × Irremediability
- **Negative potential impacts:** Scale × Scope × Irremediability × Likelihood
- **Positive actual impacts:** Scale × Scope
- **Positive potential impacts:** Scale × Scope × Likelihood

Each criterion is scored on a 1–5 scale:

| Score | Scale | Scope | Irremediability | Likelihood |
|-------|-------|-------|----------------|-----------|
| 1 | Minimal | Very local / few individuals | Easily remediable | Very unlikely (<5%) |
| 2 | Low | Local / small group | Mostly remediable | Unlikely (5–25%) |
| 3 | Moderate | Regional / significant group | Partially remediable | Possible (25–50%) |
| 4 | High | National / large group | Mostly irreversible | Likely (50–75%) |
| 5 | Severe | International / systemic | Irreversible | Very likely (>75%) |

The company must then set a materiality threshold — for example, a severity score of ≥27 for actual negative impacts (3×3×3), or ≥50 for potential negative impacts. ESRS does not prescribe a specific numerical threshold, but the threshold chosen must be justified and disclosed in ESRS 2 IRO-1.

Output: Scored and ranked impact register with impact materiality conclusions per topic.

---

### Step 4: Identify Financial Risks and Opportunities (Financial Materiality)

Working topic by topic, assess whether each ESRS sustainability matter generates financial risks or opportunities for the company. Common sources include:

- **Physical risks** — acute (flooding, extreme heat events) and chronic (sea-level rise, shifting precipitation patterns) affecting assets, supply chains, and operations
- **Transition risks** — regulatory changes (carbon pricing, product standards), technology disruption, market shifts in customer preferences, reputational effects
- **Opportunities** — energy efficiency gains, access to green finance, talent attraction through ESG leadership, new sustainable product markets

For each identified risk or opportunity, document: the type, description, time horizon (short <1 year, medium 1–5 years, long >5 years), and potential financial effect (revenue, costs, assets, liabilities, cash flows, access to finance, cost of capital).

Output: Financial risk and opportunity register per ESRS topic.

---

### Step 5: Assess Financial Significance

Rate each identified financial risk or opportunity on two dimensions:

- **Magnitude:** Could range from less than 1% of EBITDA (low) through 1–5% (medium) to more than 5% (high)
- **Likelihood:** The probability that the financial effect materialises

Combine magnitude and likelihood to reach a financial significance score. Again, the company sets its own threshold, which must be disclosed. Topics where financial significance exceeds the threshold are financially material.

Output: Scored financial risk/opportunity register with financial materiality conclusions per topic.

---

### Step 6: Determine Overall Materiality — Topic by Topic

Apply the OR logic: a topic is material if it is impact-material, financially material, or both. Compile the conclusions into a DMA summary table:

| ESRS Standard | Topic | Impact Material? | Financially Material? | Overall Material? | Reporting Required? |
|--------------|-------|-----------------|----------------------|------------------|-------------------|
| E1 | Climate Change | Yes | Yes | Yes | Full ESRS E1 |
| E2 | Pollution | No | No | No | Omit (brief statement) |
| E3 | Water & Marine Resources | Yes | Yes | Yes | Full ESRS E3 |
| E4 | Biodiversity & Ecosystems | No | No | No | Omit (brief statement) |
| S1 | Own Workforce | Yes | No | Yes | Full ESRS S1 |
| G1 | Business Conduct | No | Yes | Yes | Full ESRS G1 |

**Important note on Climate (E1):** ESRS 1 treats climate change as presumptively material for most companies. A company seeking to exclude E1 must provide robust justification and strong evidence — it is not sufficient to simply assert that climate is not relevant to the business model.

For non-material topics, the company must include a brief explanatory statement in the materiality statement — a complete absence of justification is not permitted.

---

### Step 7: Document and Disclose the DMA Process (ESRS 2 IRO-1 and SBM-3)

The DMA process itself must be disclosed, not just its outputs. Two key ESRS 2 disclosures are required:

**ESRS 2 IRO-1 — Process to identify and assess material IROs:**
- The scope of the assessment (own operations and value chain depth)
- The methodology, criteria, and thresholds used
- How stakeholders were identified and engaged
- The timeline and frequency of the DMA
- How DMA results feed into strategy and reporting decisions

**ESRS 2 SBM-3 — Material impacts, risks, and opportunities:**
- The complete list of material topics identified
- How material IROs interact with the business model and strategy
- Current and anticipated financial effects of material IROs

The materiality statement must also list each ESRS topic concluded to be non-material and provide a brief explanation for each exclusion.

---

### Step 8: Validate and Update the DMA Annually

The DMA is not a one-time exercise. It must be reviewed at least annually, and material changes to conclusions must be disclosed. Triggers for a mid-period review include:

- Significant business changes (acquisitions, new markets, new products)
- New or amended regulation affecting sustainability risks
- Emerging risks identified by stakeholders
- Material incidents in own operations or value chain

## 4. Common Pitfalls to Avoid

**Scope too narrow:** Limiting the DMA to own operations only. ESRS requires value chain consideration where material — this includes key suppliers and downstream channels.

**Stakeholder engagement limited to investors:** ESRS mandates engagement with affected stakeholders, including workers, communities, and civil society. Investor-only consultation fails this requirement.

**Financial materiality skipped:** Conducting only an impact assessment. Both perspectives must be assessed independently.

**Thresholds undocumented:** Setting arbitrary thresholds without disclosing and justifying them. ESRS 2 IRO-1 requires disclosure of the methodology and thresholds applied.

**DMA done retrospectively:** The DMA must drive reporting decisions — not be completed after the topics to report have already been chosen.

**Over-exclusion without justification:** Excluding topics with only a brief or formulaic explanation. Each exclusion requires substantive reasoning.

**Static DMA:** Treating the DMA as a one-time deliverable. Annual review is mandatory.

---

## Summary

Double materiality under CSRD is a dual-lens framework that requires companies to assess sustainability topics from both an impact perspective (the company's effects on people and the environment) and a financial perspective (sustainability matters' effects on the company). Either lens can independently trigger full reporting obligations under the relevant ESRS standard.

This is fundamentally broader than GRI, which applies only the impact lens, and fundamentally broader than TCFD, which applies only the financial lens for climate. CSRD applies both lenses across all environmental, social, and governance topics, backed by mandatory assurance and legal force as EU law.

A well-conducted DMA follows the eight-step process above: understand context, identify impacts, score impact significance, identify financial risks/opportunities, score financial significance, determine materiality topic by topic, document the process in ESRS 2 disclosures, and update annually. The DMA is the foundation on which all other CSRD reporting decisions rest.

**Key references:** ESRS 1 (paras. 19–56, esp. 45–56); ESRS 2 (IRO-1, SBM-3); Directive (EU) 2022/2464, Art. 19a; Commission Delegated Regulation (EU) 2023/2772.
