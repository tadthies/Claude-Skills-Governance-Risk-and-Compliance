# Double Materiality Under CSRD

## What Is Double Materiality?

Double materiality is the defining concept at the heart of the Corporate Sustainability Reporting Directive (CSRD) and the European Sustainability Reporting Standards (ESRS) that implement it. It holds that a company must assess materiality from two distinct but related perspectives simultaneously:

1. **Financial materiality (outside-in perspective):** How do sustainability-related risks and opportunities affect the company's financial performance, position, cash flows, and value over time? This looks at how the external world — including climate change, social instability, or resource scarcity — impacts the company.

2. **Impact materiality (inside-out perspective):** How does the company's own activities, value chain, products, and services affect people and the planet? This looks at positive and negative, actual and potential impacts on the environment and society.

A topic is material under CSRD if it clears the threshold on either dimension — it does not need to satisfy both. A company must report on it if it is material from a financial perspective, from an impact perspective, or both. When a topic is material on both axes, it sits at the intersection and represents a "double material" issue — simultaneously important to investors and to affected stakeholders.

This two-dimensional requirement is codified in ESRS 1, which lays out the general requirements for how the assessment must be conducted and documented.

## How Double Materiality Differs from GRI and TCFD

### GRI (Global Reporting Initiative)

GRI has long used an impact-first philosophy. Its standards ask companies to identify topics that reflect significant economic, environmental, and social impacts, or that substantively influence the assessments and decisions of stakeholders. The focus is primarily on the inside-out direction — what harm or benefit does the company cause in the world?

GRI 3 (2021) introduced a concept called "impact materiality" that emphasizes actual and potential impacts across the value chain. While GRI also acknowledges that stakeholder concerns can encompass financial interest, it does not formally require companies to assess how sustainability issues translate into financial risk or opportunity for the company itself. In practice, many GRI reporters focus heavily on impact and stakeholder concerns without systematically connecting those impacts to financial materiality.

**Key difference from CSRD:** GRI is primarily single-dimensional (impact-out). CSRD requires both dimensions to be assessed and reported on independently, with explicit thresholds and criteria for each.

### TCFD (Task Force on Climate-related Financial Disclosures)

TCFD operates almost entirely on the financial materiality axis. Its four pillars — Governance, Strategy, Risk Management, and Metrics and Targets — are all oriented toward understanding how climate-related risks and opportunities affect a company's financial condition and strategic prospects. TCFD was designed specifically to give investors decision-useful information about climate risk embedded in financial assets.

TCFD does not require companies to report on the impacts they have on the climate or on affected communities. A company can be fully TCFD-compliant while saying nothing about whether it is accelerating deforestation, harming local water supplies, or contributing to carbon emissions beyond what affects its own bottom line.

**Key difference from CSRD:** TCFD is single-dimensional (financial-in). CSRD requires the same financial perspective but adds a mandatory impact perspective, and it covers all sustainability topics (not only climate).

### Summary Comparison

| Framework | Direction | Scope | Who It Serves |
|-----------|-----------|-------|----------------|
| GRI | Primarily inside-out (impact) | All ESG topics | Broad stakeholders |
| TCFD | Outside-in (financial) | Climate only | Investors/capital markets |
| CSRD/ESRS | Both directions (double) | All ESG topics | Investors + broader society |

## How to Conduct a Double Materiality Assessment

The ESRS do not prescribe a single rigid method, but they do set out requirements that define the minimum standard. The following steps reflect both the regulatory requirements and established practice.

### Step 1: Define the Universe of Topics

Begin by reviewing the full list of sustainability matters covered in ESRS — environment (ESRS E1–E5 covering climate, pollution, water, biodiversity, and resource use), social (ESRS S1–S4 covering workforce, supply chain workers, affected communities, and consumers), and governance (ESRS G1 covering business conduct). These are the candidate topics. Also consider whether the company's specific sector or business model raises additional issues not fully captured in the standard list.

### Step 2: Understand the Business and Value Chain

Map the company's activities, upstream supply chain, and downstream use and end-of-life stages. This scoping exercise identifies where the company has direct operations, where it has significant business relationships, and where its products or services have effects. The value chain scope is important because CSRD requires consideration of impacts and risks that extend beyond the company's own legal boundary.

### Step 3: Engage Stakeholders

Stakeholder engagement is not optional under CSRD. The company must gather input from those who are affected by its activities (workers, communities, suppliers, customers) and from those who use sustainability information for financial decisions (investors, lenders, analysts). Engagement methods can include surveys, interviews, focus groups, materiality workshops, and review of public disclosures and analyst reports. The results inform both the impact and financial assessments.

### Step 4: Assess Impact Materiality (Inside-Out)

For each candidate topic, evaluate:

- **Severity of actual negative impacts:** Considering scale (how serious is the harm?), scope (how many people or how much of the environment is affected?), and remediability (can the harm be undone?).
- **Severity of potential negative impacts:** Assessed on the same three dimensions, weighted by the likelihood that the impact will occur.
- **Scale of actual and potential positive impacts:** Positive impacts are also assessed, though negative impacts receive more weight in the severity calculus.

Topics that cross a defined severity threshold (which the company sets and must justify) are material from an impact perspective. The threshold should be consistent across topics and documented.

### Step 5: Assess Financial Materiality (Outside-In)

For each candidate topic, evaluate whether it gives rise to material risks or opportunities for the company over the short, medium, or long term. Relevant considerations include:

- Physical risks (acute events or chronic changes in environmental conditions that affect assets, operations, or supply chains)
- Transition risks (regulatory, market, reputational, or technological changes associated with moving toward a more sustainable economy)
- Dependencies on natural or social capital that could be disrupted
- Opportunities such as access to new markets, green financing, or improved customer loyalty

A topic is financially material if it has or could reasonably be expected to have a material effect on the company's financial position, performance, cash flows, or cost of capital. The definition aligns with what a reasonable investor would consider significant.

### Step 6: Apply Thresholds and Determine Materiality

Combine the results. A topic is material under CSRD if it clears the threshold on either or both dimensions. The company must document:

- Which topics are material, and why
- Which topics were considered but assessed as not material, and why
- The thresholds and criteria used for each dimension
- How stakeholder input was incorporated

The final materiality list determines which ESRS disclosure requirements apply. Some disclosures in ESRS 2 (general disclosures) are mandatory regardless of materiality. For all other topic-specific ESRS, the disclosure requirements are conditional on the company having assessed the topic as material.

### Step 7: Validate and Review

The assessment should be reviewed by senior management and the board, since CSRD places governance responsibility for sustainability reporting at the board level. It should also be reviewed periodically — at least annually — and updated when material changes occur in the business or its context. In many EU member states, the sustainability statement (including the materiality assessment rationale) will be subject to limited assurance from an auditor, with a trajectory toward reasonable assurance.

### Step 8: Document and Disclose

The company must include in its sustainability statement:

- A description of the process used to conduct the double materiality assessment
- The outcome (list of material topics)
- A summary of why certain topics were or were not assessed as material
- The role of stakeholder engagement in shaping the outcome

Transparency in the process is itself a disclosure requirement. A company cannot simply publish a list of material topics without explaining how it arrived at them.

## Practical Considerations

**Threshold-setting is the hardest part.** ESRS do not specify a universal quantitative threshold. Companies must exercise judgment and disclose their reasoning. Inconsistent thresholds across topics, or thresholds that are set so high that almost nothing is material, are likely to attract scrutiny from auditors and regulators.

**The value chain requirement adds significant scope.** Many companies find that their most significant impacts — particularly for social topics like labor rights — sit in the supply chain rather than in direct operations. Conducting meaningful due diligence across Tier 1 and beyond requires investment in supplier engagement and data collection.

**Financial and impact materiality can interact.** A topic that starts as impact material (a company harms a local community) can become financially material over time as litigation risk, regulatory action, or reputational damage materializes. The double materiality framework is dynamic, not a one-time exercise.

**CSRD applies to a much larger population than TCFD or GRI.** CSRD is mandatory for large EU companies and EU-listed SMEs (with different timelines), and it extends to non-EU companies with significant EU turnover. For companies already reporting under GRI or TCFD, the double materiality requirement means extending and formalizing an assessment that may previously have been done informally or from only one direction.
