# CSRD Disclosure Requirements Based on Your Double Materiality Assessment

## Overview

Under the Corporate Sustainability Reporting Directive (CSRD) and the European Sustainability Reporting Standards (ESRS), companies only need to provide detailed disclosures for topics that are material. Your double materiality assessment identified three material topics: Climate Change (ESRS E1), Own Workforce (ESRS S1), and Business Conduct (ESRS G1). Non-material topics do not require full standard-level disclosures, though you must document and explain your materiality conclusions.

The following outlines what you must disclose.

---

## 1. Cross-Cutting / General Disclosures (Always Required)

Regardless of materiality outcomes, ESRS 1 and ESRS 2 apply to all companies in scope of CSRD. These are not subject to materiality — they form the mandatory baseline.

### ESRS 1 (General Requirements)
This standard sets out the architecture, principles, and methodology for sustainability reporting. It does not itself contain disclosure requirements but governs how you apply the other standards.

### ESRS 2 (General Disclosures) — Fully Mandatory
You must disclose all requirements under ESRS 2, which covers:

- **Governance (GOV)**: Governance bodies' oversight of sustainability matters, management roles and responsibilities, integration of sustainability in corporate governance
- **Strategy (SBM)**: Business model and value chain description, sustainability strategy and significant groups of stakeholders, material impacts, risks and opportunities and how they interact with strategy and business model
- **Impact, Risk and Opportunity Management (IRO)**: The process used to identify, assess, prioritise and monitor material IROs — including a full description of your double materiality assessment process
- **Metrics and Targets (MT)**: Disclosure of the sustainability-related targets set and performance against those targets

ESRS 2 also requires you to disclose the **list of material sustainability matters** identified through your assessment, and critically, an **explanation of why topics assessed as non-material were excluded**. This means you must document why Pollution (E2), Water (E3), Biodiversity (E4), Circular Economy (E5), Value Chain Workers (S2), Communities (S3), and Consumers/End-users (S4) were determined non-material.

## 2. Material Topic Disclosures

For the three topics that came out as material, you must apply the relevant topical ESRS standards. Each topical standard contains a mix of mandatory disclosures and disclosures subject to materiality.

---

### ESRS E1 — Climate Change

This is one of the most demanding ESRS standards. Key disclosure areas include:

**Governance and Strategy**
- Transition plan for climate change mitigation aligned with Paris Agreement / 1.5°C pathway
- Climate-related physical and transition risks and opportunities
- Resilience of business model and strategy under climate scenarios (scenario analysis)
- Integration of climate into overall strategy and financial planning

**Impact, Risk and Opportunity Management**
- Policies for climate change mitigation and adaptation
- Actions and resources allocated to manage climate-related IROs
- Engagement with value chain on climate matters

**Metrics and Targets**
- **Scope 1, 2, and 3 greenhouse gas (GHG) emissions** — this is a core mandatory metric; Scope 3 can be phased in for some companies
- GHG removal and storage activities
- GHG intensity metrics
- Energy consumption and mix (renewable vs. non-renewable)
- Climate-related targets, including whether aligned with science-based targets
- Potential financial effects of material climate risks

Note: ESRS E1 includes some of the most prescriptive quantitative requirements in the entire framework, particularly around GHG accounting methodology (aligned with GHG Protocol).

---

### ESRS S1 — Own Workforce

This standard covers your employees and non-employee workers (e.g., contractors working under your direction).

**Governance and Strategy**
- Policies related to your own workforce (e.g., fair wages, health and safety, non-discrimination, working conditions)
- How you engage with your own workforce on material topics

**Impact, Risk and Opportunity Management**
- Processes for identifying and managing impacts on your own workforce
- Remediation processes for identified negative impacts
- Channels for workers to raise concerns

**Metrics and Targets**
- **Headcount data**: total workforce broken down by gender, country, employment type (permanent/temporary, full-time/part-time), non-employees
- **Health and safety**: work-related injuries, ill health, and fatalities (rates and absolute numbers)
- **Work-related health and safety management system** coverage
- **Remuneration**: pay gap metrics (gender pay gap), ratio of CEO pay to median employee pay
- Collective bargaining coverage
- Training and skills development metrics
- Diversity metrics (particularly gender across governance and management levels)
- Cases of discrimination, harassment, and outcomes

Several S1 metrics are mandatory even if the underlying sub-topic might have limited materiality; others apply only where the relevant sub-topic (e.g., remuneration adequacy, work-life balance) is material.

---

### ESRS G1 — Business Conduct

This standard covers ethics, anti-corruption, anti-bribery, lobbying, supplier relationships, and payment practices.

**Governance and Strategy**
- Corporate culture and conduct policies (code of conduct, anti-corruption and anti-bribery policies)
- Whistleblower/speak-up mechanisms
- Political engagement and lobbying activities
- Protection of whistleblowers

**Impact, Risk and Opportunity Management**
- Processes for preventing and detecting corruption and bribery
- Management of relationships with suppliers and payment terms

**Metrics and Targets**
- Confirmed incidents of corruption or bribery
- Legal actions related to anti-competitive behaviour
- Payment practices: average payment period to suppliers, proportion of payments made beyond agreed terms (particularly relevant for SME suppliers)
- Fines and settlements for ethical violations

## 3. Non-Material Topics — What You Still Need to Do

Even though Pollution (E2), Water (E3), Biodiversity (E4), Circular Economy (E5), Value Chain Workers (S2), Communities (S3), and Consumers/End-users (S4) are non-material, CSRD/ESRS imposes the following obligations:

1. **Document your assessment**: ESRS 2 requires you to describe the process and outcome of your double materiality assessment, including the rationale for exclusions.

2. **Disclose that these topics were assessed and found non-material**: Your sustainability statement should include a clear statement noting which ESRS topics were assessed and why they did not meet the materiality threshold.

3. **Revisit periodically**: Materiality is not static. You should reassess each reporting period. Changes in business model, supply chains, or operating context could make currently non-material topics material in the future.

4. **Sector-specific standards**: If sector-specific ESRS are finalized and applicable to your sector, they may require certain disclosures regardless of your materiality outcome — monitor EFRAG's work on sector standards.

5. **Phased-in requirements**: Some E1 and S1 metrics have transitional provisions allowing phase-in over the first 1–3 years of reporting. Check ESRS Appendix C for the specific list of phase-in reliefs.

## 5. Practical Considerations

- **Connectivity to financial statements**: ESRS requires disclosures to be connected to your financial reporting where sustainability risks and impacts have financial implications. This is particularly relevant for E1 climate risks.

- **Value chain**: Even though S2–S4 are non-material, ESRS E1 and S1 may still require some consideration of your upstream supply chain (particularly for Scope 3 emissions under E1).

- **Assurance**: CSRD requires limited assurance of the sustainability statement (moving toward reasonable assurance over time). Your disclosures must be audit-ready.

- **Location**: The sustainability statement must be included in the management report (directors' report) as a dedicated section — it cannot be a standalone document.

- **Digital tagging**: Disclosures must be tagged using the European Single Electronic Format (ESEF) with the ESRS XBRL taxonomy once the taxonomy is finalized and applicable to your reporting period.
