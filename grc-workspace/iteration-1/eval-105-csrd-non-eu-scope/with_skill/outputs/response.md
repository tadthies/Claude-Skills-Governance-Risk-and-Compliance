## Scope / Threshold Analysis

### Step 1: Non-EU Company Threshold Test (Art. 40a CSRD)

CSRD Art. 40a (transposed from Directive 2013/34/EU as amended) extends the directive's reach to non-EU parent undertakings that generate significant EU revenue and have qualifying EU-based entities.

| Criterion | Threshold | Your Position | Met? |
|-----------|-----------|---------------|------|
| Net turnover generated in the EU | > €150M | €200M combined from German and French subsidiaries | **YES** |
| Qualifying EU subsidiary (large company OR listed) | ≥ 1 large EU subsidiary or branch with >€40M EU turnover | German subsidiary: 600 employees (see Step 2) | **YES** |

**Verdict: Both criteria are satisfied. CSRD applies to your group.**

### Step 2: German Subsidiary — Large Company Test (Art. 3(4) Directive 2013/34/EU)

A company qualifies as "large" under EU law if it exceeds **at least 2 of the following 3 thresholds**:

| Size Criterion | Threshold | German Subsidiary |
|----------------|-----------|-------------------|
| Number of employees | > 250 | **600** — Exceeds |
| Net turnover | > €40M | Not stated, but likely exceeded given total EU revenue of €200M |
| Total assets | > €20M | Not stated |

With 600 employees, the German subsidiary already exceeds one threshold definitively. Given it is part of a group generating €200M in EU revenue, it almost certainly also exceeds the €40M turnover threshold. If so, it qualifies as a **large EU subsidiary**, satisfying the second prong of the Art. 40a test.

**If the German subsidiary's standalone turnover exceeds €40M (highly probable given group revenues), it qualifies as a large company even without being listed.**

The French subsidiary further reinforces the group's EU footprint.

## What CSRD Requires from You

### Who Files the Report?

Under Art. 40a, the **EU subsidiaries themselves** (specifically, the large ones — here, the German subsidiary and potentially the French subsidiary) are obligated to publish a CSRD-compliant sustainability report. There is no option for the US parent to file a consolidated ESRS report at parent level as a substitute — the obligation sits at the EU subsidiary level unless an equivalent third-country parent report is accepted (see "Group Reporting" below).

**Practical implication:** Your German subsidiary is the primary obligated entity. If the French subsidiary also qualifies as large, it bears its own obligation.

### Reporting Standard

Non-EU company subsidiaries report under the **ESRS standards** (Commission Delegated Regulation (EU) 2023/2772) — the same standards as EU large companies. There is a sector-agnostic set of ESRS for non-EU undertakings currently being developed by EFRAG (the "ESRS for third-country undertakings"), though EU subsidiaries of non-EU groups will use the standard ESRS unless a specific equivalence decision has been made.

## Key Obligations — What You Need to Do

### 1. Double Materiality Assessment (DMA) — The Starting Point

Before any disclosure can be prepared, the German (and French) subsidiary must conduct a **Double Materiality Assessment** (ESRS 1, paras. 45–56). This determines which ESRS topical standards apply.

**Two lenses:**
- **Impact materiality:** Does the subsidiary's technology operations cause actual or potential impacts on people or the environment (e.g., digital waste, energy use in data centres, workforce conditions)?
- **Financial materiality:** Do sustainability factors create risks or opportunities for the subsidiary's financial performance (e.g., physical climate risk to EU offices, regulatory cost from carbon pricing)?

A topic is material if it meets **either** lens. As a technology company, the DMA will likely identify climate (energy, GHG from data centres), workforce (ESRS S1), and business conduct (ESRS G1) as highly probable material topics.

### 2. Mandatory Disclosures — ESRS 2 (All In-Scope Companies)

Regardless of DMA outcomes, **ESRS 2 General Disclosures are mandatory** for all in-scope entities. These cover:

| Disclosure | Content Required |
|------------|-----------------|
| **GOV-1** | Role of board/supervisory bodies in sustainability oversight |
| **GOV-2** | Management's role and sustainability expertise |
| **GOV-3** | Sustainability-linked incentive schemes |
| **GOV-4** | Due diligence statement (alignment with UN Guiding Principles, OECD Guidelines) |
| **GOV-5** | Risk management and internal controls for sustainability |
| **SBM-1** | Business model, strategy, and value chain description |
| **SBM-2** | Interests and views of stakeholders |
| **SBM-3** | Material impacts, risks, and opportunities (output of DMA) |
| **IRO-1** | Process for identifying and assessing material IROs |

### 3. Likely Material Topical Standards for a Technology Company

| ESRS Standard | Topic | Likely Applicability |
|---------------|-------|---------------------|
| **ESRS E1** | Climate Change | High — data centres, office energy consumption, business travel, Scope 3 supply chain emissions |
| **ESRS S1** | Own Workforce | High — 600+ employees in Germany alone; pay gap, collective bargaining, H&S, training |
| **ESRS G1** | Business Conduct | High — anti-corruption, data ethics, supplier practices |
| **ESRS E5** | Resource Use & Circular Economy | Medium — hardware lifecycle, e-waste |
| **ESRS S4** | Consumers & End-Users | Medium — data privacy, product safety obligations |
| **ESRS S2** | Workers in Value Chain | Medium — supply chain labour practices |

Note: The DMA will determine the definitive list.

### 4. Key Datapoints to Prepare (Illustrative for E1 and S1)

**ESRS E1 — Climate (if material):**
- Scope 1 GHG emissions (direct, e.g., fleet, on-site combustion)
- Scope 2 GHG emissions (electricity — both market-based and location-based)
- Scope 3 GHG emissions across all 15 categories (especially Categories 1, 3, 6, 11 for tech companies)
- Energy consumption and renewable energy share
- GHG reduction targets (Paris-aligned, 2030/2050)
- Climate transition plan (Art. 19a(2)(a))
- EU Taxonomy eligible and aligned revenue/capex/opex

**ESRS S1 — Own Workforce (if material):**
- Total headcount by gender, contract type, country
- Gender pay gap (aligned with EU Pay Transparency Directive)
- % employees covered by collective bargaining agreements
- Work-related injury rate (LTIFR) and fatalities
- Training hours per employee
- Employee turnover rate

### 5. Value Chain Reporting

CSRD requires consideration of the **upstream and downstream value chain** where material (ESRS 1). As a technology company, this means:

- **Upstream:** Hardware suppliers, cloud infrastructure partners, component manufacturers
- **Downstream:** Customer use-phase emissions (Scope 3 Category 11), end-of-life product disposal

Where supplier data is unavailable, proxy data and sector averages may be used, but the methodology and limitations must be disclosed.

### 6. Group Reporting — Can the US Parent File on Behalf of Subsidiaries?

Under Art. 19a(9) and related provisions, a subsidiary may be **exempt from its own CSRD filing** if:
- It is included in a **consolidated sustainability report** prepared by a parent undertaking, AND
- That parent's report complies with CSRD or an **equivalent standard** as determined by the European Commission

Currently, **no third-country standard has been formally declared equivalent** by the Commission. This means:
- A US parent reporting under GRI, TCFD, or SEC climate disclosure rules alone **cannot substitute** for CSRD compliance at the EU subsidiary level
- However, if the Commission declares equivalence for a US framework in the future, this option may become available

**Practical path:** The German subsidiary (and French subsidiary if large) must file their own ESRS-compliant reports unless and until equivalence is formally granted.

### 7. Report Format and Assurance Requirements

| Requirement | Details |
|-------------|---------|
| **Location** | Dedicated section of the German subsidiary's management report (Lagesbericht) — not a standalone sustainability document |

## Critical Considerations and Risks

### 1. French Subsidiary Threshold Confirmation
The question does not specify the French subsidiary's employee count or standalone turnover. If the French subsidiary also qualifies as a large company (≥2 of: >250 employees, >€40M turnover, >€20M assets), it bears **its own independent CSRD obligation**. Confirm the French subsidiary's size classification immediately.

### 2. German Subsidiary Standalone Turnover
Confirm that the German subsidiary's standalone turnover exceeds €40M. While highly probable given the group's €200M EU revenue, this should be formally verified to ensure the Art. 40a trigger is met.

### 3. CSRD Omnibus Package (2025)
The European Commission proposed a simplification package in February 2025 (the "CSRD Omnibus") that may:
- Narrow the scope of companies subject to CSRD
- Reduce the number of mandatory datapoints
- Potentially raise thresholds

As of the current date (May 2026), monitor the legislative status of the Omnibus Package. The FY 2028 timeline and current ESRS obligations remain the baseline until formal changes are enacted.

### 4. German Codetermination and Works Council
The German subsidiary's 600 employees will trigger German codetermination rules (Mitbestimmungsgesetz). Any CSRD-related data collection on workforce matters (ESRS S1) should be coordinated with the **Betriebsrat (works council)**, as workforce data collection may require consultation or agreement under German labour law.

### 5. Intragroup Data Sharing
The US parent will need to consider **data governance arrangements** for sharing sustainability data between the parent and EU subsidiaries. This intersects with GDPR obligations for any personal data elements (e.g., headcount by gender, pay gap data). Ensure data transfer mechanisms are in place.
