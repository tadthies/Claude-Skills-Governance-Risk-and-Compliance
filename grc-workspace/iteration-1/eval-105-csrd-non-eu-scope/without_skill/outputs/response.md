# CSRD Applicability for a US-Headquartered Company with EU Subsidiaries

## Summary Answer

Yes, CSRD likely applies to your situation — but the obligations fall on your EU subsidiaries, not directly on the US parent company. Based on the facts provided, your German subsidiary almost certainly qualifies as a large undertaking under CSRD and will be required to publish a sustainability report. There is also a potential obligation at the consolidated (group) level if the EU group as a whole meets the thresholds.

---

## 1. CSRD Background and Scope

The Corporate Sustainability Reporting Directive (CSRD), adopted in December 2022 and effective from January 2023, significantly expands the EU's non-financial reporting requirements. It replaces the Non-Financial Reporting Directive (NFRD) and is implemented into national law by EU member states (Germany and France have both transposed it).

CSRD applies to:
- Large EU companies (including subsidiaries of non-EU groups)
- Non-EU parent companies with significant EU activity (via a separate "third-country undertaking" rule)
- EU-listed SMEs (with a later opt-out period)

---

## 2. Does CSRD Apply to You as a Non-EU Company?

### Direct Obligation on the US Parent (Third-Country Undertaking Rule)

Under Article 40 of CSRD (the "third-country undertaking" provision), a non-EU parent company is directly subject to CSRD reporting if it meets **both** of the following thresholds:

- **Net turnover in the EU exceeds €150 million** for each of the last two consecutive financial years, AND
- The company has either:
  - At least one EU subsidiary that is a large undertaking or a listed SME, OR
  - At least one EU branch generating more than €40 million net turnover

**Your situation:** With €200M in combined EU revenue, you exceed the €150M EU turnover threshold. You also have at least one EU subsidiary (the German subsidiary with 600 employees), which, as shown below, qualifies as a large undertaking.

**Conclusion:** The US parent company is subject to the third-country undertaking rule and must publish a group-level sustainability report covering EU operations. This report must be filed by a designated EU subsidiary (typically the largest one, in your case likely the German subsidiary) and must comply with the European Sustainability Reporting Standards (ESRS) or an equivalent standard recognized by the European Commission.

**Timing for third-country undertakings:** The obligation for non-EU parent companies applies for financial years starting on or after **1 January 2028** (with first reports due in 2029), under the current CSRD timeline. Note that the EU has proposed and in some cases adopted deferrals — you should confirm the current timeline as it may have been adjusted.

---

### Obligation on the German Subsidiary (Large Undertaking Rule)

Independently of the parent company's obligations, each EU subsidiary must be assessed against the CSRD thresholds for large undertakings. A company qualifies as a "large undertaking" under EU Accounting Directive criteria if it exceeds **at least two of the three** following thresholds:

| Criterion | Threshold |
|---|---|
| Balance sheet total | > €25 million |
| Net turnover | > €50 million |
| Average number of employees | > 250 |

**Your German subsidiary:**
- Employees: 600 (exceeds the 250 threshold)
- Revenue: The combined EU revenue is €200M across Germany and France. Even if split, it is highly likely the German subsidiary alone exceeds €50M net turnover given the scale of operations.
- Balance sheet: Not stated, but a technology company with 600 employees and >€50M revenue would typically exceed €25M in balance sheet total.

**Conclusion:** The German subsidiary almost certainly meets two or more thresholds and qualifies as a large undertaking under CSRD. It is therefore independently required to report under CSRD.

**Timing for large undertakings:** For companies that were not previously subject to NFRD (non-public-interest entities), the obligation applies for financial years starting on or after **1 January 2025** (with first reports due in 2026). However, given recent EU "Omnibus" simplification proposals in early 2025, this timeline may have been deferred — confirm the current applicable dates.

**French subsidiary:** Similarly, the French subsidiary should be assessed against the same thresholds independently.

## 3. What Do You Need to Do?

### Step 1: Confirm Subsidiary-Level Thresholds

- Obtain the precise revenue, balance sheet, and headcount figures for both the German and French subsidiaries separately.
- Determine whether each subsidiary independently meets at least two of the three large undertaking thresholds.
- If both subsidiaries qualify, each may need its own CSRD-compliant sustainability report (unless a consolidated report at the EU sub-group level is permitted and elected).

### Step 2: Assess the Third-Country Undertaking Obligation

- Confirm that combined EU net turnover has exceeded €150M for each of the past two consecutive financial years.
- Identify which EU subsidiary will be designated to file the group sustainability report on behalf of the US parent.
- Track the third-country undertaking reporting timeline (currently financial years from 1 January 2028).

### Step 3: Determine Reporting Structure

You have two main structural options:

**Option A — Subsidiary-Level Reports Only**
Each qualifying subsidiary reports individually. This is simpler but may result in duplicative effort and does not satisfy the third-country undertaking obligation at the parent level.

**Option B — Consolidated EU Sub-Group Report**
A designated EU subsidiary (e.g., the German subsidiary) publishes a consolidated sustainability report covering all EU subsidiaries. This can satisfy both the large undertaking obligation and (from 2028) the third-country undertaking obligation, provided the report meets the required standards.

### Step 4: Align with European Sustainability Reporting Standards (ESRS)

CSRD reports must follow the ESRS, developed by EFRAG (European Financial Reporting Advisory Group). Key ESRS requirements include:

- **Double materiality assessment:** Identify sustainability topics that are material both from a financial perspective (how ESG issues affect the company) and an impact perspective (how the company affects people and the environment).
- **Mandatory disclosures:** Certain general disclosures (ESRS 2) are mandatory for all reporting entities.
- **Topical standards:** Additional disclosures on climate (ESRS E1), pollution, biodiversity, water, workers, affected communities, business conduct, and governance — required based on materiality assessment outcomes.
- **Value chain disclosures:** You must report on sustainability matters across your upstream and downstream value chain where material.

### Step 5: Third-Party Assurance

CSRD requires **limited assurance** on sustainability reports from an independent auditor, with a pathway to reasonable assurance in future years. Engage your statutory auditor or an accredited third-party assurance provider early.

### Step 6: Integration with Annual Report

CSRD requires the sustainability report to be published as a dedicated section of the management report (annual report) — it cannot be a standalone document separate from the financial reporting package.

### Step 7: XBRL Tagging

Sustainability disclosures must be tagged in XBRL (iXBRL) format using the ESRS taxonomy to enable machine-readable reporting and submission via the European Single Electronic Format (ESEF).

### Step 8: Governance and Data Infrastructure

- Appoint a CSRD project owner at the subsidiary level (and group level for the parent).
- Map data collection responsibilities across business units: HR, operations, procurement, finance, legal.
- Implement a sustainability data management system capable of collecting and auditing ESG metrics.
- Update board-level governance to include oversight of sustainability reporting.

## 5. Practical Recommendations

1. **Engage EU legal counsel** in Germany and France immediately to confirm national transposition specifics and any local regulatory guidance.
2. **Conduct a double materiality assessment** as the foundation for all ESRS disclosures — this is time-consuming and should begin 12–18 months before the first report is due.
3. **Assess whether the ESRS or an equivalent standard** (e.g., GRI, if recognized by the Commission) will be used for the third-country undertaking report.
4. **Coordinate reporting across subsidiaries** to avoid inconsistent disclosures and reduce duplication.
5. **Engage your auditor early** on assurance scope, methodology, and readiness requirements.
6. **Monitor legislative developments** — the Omnibus package may materially change your obligations and timelines.

## Conclusion

CSRD applies to your organization in two ways: (1) your German subsidiary is independently required to report as a large EU undertaking, and likely your French subsidiary as well; and (2) the US parent company is subject to the third-country undertaking rule given €200M in EU revenue and the presence of large EU subsidiaries. The immediate compliance priority is preparing the German (and potentially French) subsidiary for their large undertaking reporting obligations. Planning for the parent-level consolidated report should begin now given the complexity of ESRS implementation.
