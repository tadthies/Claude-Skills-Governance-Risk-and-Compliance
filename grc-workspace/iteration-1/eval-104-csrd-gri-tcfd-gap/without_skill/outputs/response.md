# Transitioning from GRI Standards and TCFD to CSRD/ESRS: Gap Analysis and Effort Assessment

## Executive Summary

If your organization already reports under GRI Standards and TCFD, you have a meaningful head start on CSRD/ESRS compliance. However, transitioning is not a simple mapping exercise. CSRD/ESRS introduces a more prescriptive, auditable, and legally binding framework with significant new requirements that neither GRI nor TCFD fully address. Expect a moderate-to-substantial effort depending on your organization's size, sector, and current reporting maturity.

---

## What You Already Have: Your Starting Advantages

### From GRI Standards
GRI provides a strong foundation for the social and governance dimensions of ESRS. Key areas of alignment include:

- **Stakeholder engagement processes** — GRI's materiality approach involves stakeholder dialogue, which partially supports ESRS's double materiality requirements.
- **Social disclosures** — GRI 400-series (Labor, Human Rights, Society) maps reasonably well to ESRS S1 (Own Workforce), S2 (Workers in the Value Chain), S3 (Affected Communities), and S4 (Consumers and End-users).
- **Governance disclosures** — GRI 2 (General Disclosures) overlaps with ESRS G1 (Business Conduct) and ESRS 2 governance disclosures.
- **Environmental topic coverage** — GRI 300-series (Energy, Water, Emissions, Waste, Biodiversity) provides baseline data that feeds into ESRS E1–E5.
- **Reporting infrastructure** — Existing data collection systems, internal controls, and reporting workflows can be adapted rather than built from scratch.

### From TCFD
TCFD was the primary precursor to ESRS E1 (Climate Change) and provides strong alignment for:

- **Climate governance** — Board and management oversight of climate risk maps directly to ESRS 2 GOV disclosures.
- **Risk and opportunity identification** — TCFD's risk/opportunity framework aligns with ESRS E1's physical and transition risk disclosures.
- **Scenario analysis** — If you have conducted TCFD scenario analysis, this work is directly reusable for ESRS E1 requirements on climate scenario analysis.
- **Metrics and targets** — Scope 1, 2, and 3 GHG emissions, and climate-related financial metrics, are required in both frameworks.

## Key Gaps: Where Significant Work Is Still Required

### 1. Double Materiality Assessment (Critical Gap)
**Effort: High**

This is the single most significant structural gap. ESRS requires a rigorous **double materiality assessment** that evaluates:
- **Impact materiality**: How does your company affect people and the environment (positive and negative, actual and potential, across the value chain)?
- **Financial materiality**: How do sustainability matters affect your company's financial position and performance?

GRI uses a single-axis stakeholder-informed materiality process. TCFD uses financial materiality only. ESRS requires both simultaneously and with documented, auditable methodology. This assessment determines which ESRS topic standards apply to you and must be defensible to auditors. Most organizations undergoing CSRD transition find this to be their most time-consuming workload.

### 2. Value Chain Disclosures (Significant Gap)
**Effort: High**

ESRS extends disclosure requirements far into the upstream and downstream value chain — suppliers, contractors, distribution partners, and end customers. GRI encourages but does not mandate this depth. TCFD is primarily focused on the reporting entity itself. You will likely need:
- Supplier data collection programs or surveys
- Contractual requirements with key suppliers
- Estimation methodologies where direct data is unavailable
- Governance frameworks for value chain oversight

### 3. Biodiversity and Ecosystems — ESRS E4 (Significant Gap)
**Effort: Medium to High**

Unless you have voluntarily adopted TNFD or similar frameworks, biodiversity is likely a major gap. ESRS E4 requires:
- Site-specific assessments of biodiversity-sensitive locations
- Metrics on land use, species impact, and ecosystem degradation
- Alignment with EU Biodiversity Strategy targets

GRI 304 (Biodiversity) exists but is rarely reported with the granularity ESRS E4 demands. TCFD does not address biodiversity.

### 4. Resource Use and Circular Economy — ESRS E5 (Moderate Gap)
**Effort: Medium**

ESRS E5 on resource use and circular economy goes beyond what GRI 301 (Materials) and GRI 306 (Waste) typically cover, requiring:
- Inflows and outflows of resources tracked against circular economy principles
- Products and materials designed for reuse, repair, and recyclability
- Alignment with EU Circular Economy Action Plan targets

### 5. Own Workforce Detail — ESRS S1 (Moderate Gap)
**Effort: Medium**

ESRS S1 is substantially more granular than most GRI reporters achieve in practice. New requirements include:
- Detailed breakdown of workforce by employment type, contract type, gender, age, and region
- Living wage assessments and coverage rates
- More specific health and safety metrics
- Collective bargaining and freedom of association data at a greater level of specificity
- Disclosures on non-employee workers (contractors, agency workers)

### 6. Narrative and Policy Disclosures (Moderate Gap)
**Effort: Medium**

Each ESRS topic standard requires a structured set of disclosures covering:
- **Policies** in place addressing the topic
- **Actions and resources** allocated
- **Targets** set and progress against them
- **Metrics** (both mandatory and entity-specific)

GRI reporters often have partial versions of these, but ESRS requires them to follow a specific, mandatory structure. Retrofitting existing narrative disclosures to this architecture takes significant editorial and governance effort.

### 7. Assurance Readiness (Significant Gap for Most)
**Effort: Medium to High**

CSRD mandates **limited assurance** from the outset, with a trajectory toward **reasonable assurance**. This requires:
- Internal controls over sustainability reporting comparable to financial reporting controls
- Audit trails and data documentation
- Cross-functional governance (sustainability, finance, legal, operations)
- Engagement with external auditors who will scrutinize not just disclosures but data processes

Most GRI and TCFD reporters have not built sustainability data infrastructure to this standard. This is often the area that requires the most investment in systems and process redesign.

### 8. ESRS 2 — General Disclosures (Moderate Gap)
**Effort: Medium**

ESRS 2 is mandatory for all in-scope entities regardless of double materiality outcomes. It requires structured disclosures on:
- Governance bodies and their sustainability roles
- Management of sustainability-related impacts, risks, and opportunities
- Strategy and business model integration
- Stakeholder engagement processes (more detailed than GRI's approach)
- The double materiality assessment methodology itself

While GRI 2 and TCFD governance disclosures cover some of this ground, ESRS 2 has a more prescriptive format that will require dedicated structuring work.

## Areas Where Gaps Are Minimal

| Topic | Reason |
|-------|--------|
| Climate governance (ESRS E1 GOV) | Strong TCFD coverage |
| Scope 1 and 2 GHG emissions | Covered by both GRI 305 and TCFD |
| Transition risks and opportunities | TCFD scenario analysis directly applicable |
| Labor practices (partial) | GRI 400-series provides strong base |
| Anti-corruption and business ethics | GRI 205/206 maps well to ESRS G1 |
| Water and effluents | GRI 303 provides reasonable base for ESRS E3 |

## Effort Estimation by Phase

| Phase | Description | Estimated Effort |
|-------|-------------|-----------------|
| **Double Materiality Assessment** | Methodology design, stakeholder engagement, topic prioritization | 3–6 months |
| **Gap Analysis and Mapping** | Detailed mapping of existing GRI/TCFD disclosures to ESRS requirements | 1–2 months |
| **Data Architecture** | Systems and process design for new data points (value chain, biodiversity, circular economy) | 6–12 months |
| **Policy and Governance Updates** | Board-level oversight structures, internal policies aligned to ESRS topics | 3–6 months |
| **Narrative Restructuring** | Reformatting existing disclosures to ESRS architecture | 2–4 months |
| **Assurance Preparation** | Internal controls, documentation, pre-assurance readiness review | 3–6 months |
| **First Report Production** | Drafting, review, and finalization | 3–4 months |

**Total indicative timeline: 12–24 months** for a well-resourced organization, depending on scope and starting maturity. Organizations with strong GRI and TCFD programs are typically at the lower end of this range.

---

## Key Recommendations

1. **Start with the double materiality assessment.** Everything else in ESRS is conditioned on its outputs. Invest in a rigorous, documented methodology early.

2. **Do a structured GRI-to-ESRS mapping exercise.** Many GRI disclosures have direct or partial equivalents in ESRS. Identify what you can reuse and what needs enhancement versus what must be built from scratch.

3. **Treat TCFD as your ESRS E1 foundation.** Your climate scenario analysis, risk identification, and metrics work is directly transferable — focus on filling gaps (e.g., physical risk at asset level, Scope 3 completeness, transition plan requirements under ESRS E1).

4. **Prioritize value chain data collection.** This typically has the longest lead time and requires external partner engagement. Start scoping supplier data programs early.

5. **Engage your assurance provider early.** Understanding what your auditors will expect shapes how you build data processes. Early engagement prevents costly rework.

6. **Assess ESRS E4 (Biodiversity) carefully.** If your double materiality assessment flags biodiversity as material, this is likely your largest net-new disclosure workload.

7. **Invest in data infrastructure.** CSRD-grade reporting requires sustainability data to be managed with the same rigor as financial data. This often means upgrading ESG data management systems.

## Summary Assessment

| Dimension | Assessment |
|-----------|-----------|
| Overall transition effort | Moderate to Substantial |
| Biggest single gap | Double Materiality Assessment methodology |
| Largest data gap | Value chain and biodiversity data |
| Largest structural gap | Assurance-ready internal controls |
| Biggest advantage from GRI | Social and governance topic coverage |
| Biggest advantage from TCFD | Climate risk, governance, and scenario analysis |
| Estimated timeline | 12–24 months to first compliant report |

Organizations that have invested seriously in GRI and TCFD reporting are meaningfully better positioned than those starting from scratch, but should not underestimate the structural and legal shift that CSRD represents. The combination of mandatory assurance, double materiality, and the sheer scope of ESRS topic standards makes this a significant compliance program, not merely a reporting reformat.
